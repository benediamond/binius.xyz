# The Procedure [A description of our multilinear commitment scheme]

We've already explained in rough detail the [idea of error-correcting codes](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/codes), as well as our most important _particular_ code, the [binary-field Reed–Solomon code](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/additive). At this point, we're ready to describe Binius's multilinear polynomial commitment procedure.

Again, the problem is to describe what we should _do_ to a multilinear polynomial in order to commit it. To fix notation, we again pick an integer $\ell \geq 0$ and a huge tower field $\mathcal{T}_\tau$. Our input thus will be an $\ell$-variate multilinear $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) \in \mathcal{T}_\tau[X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}]^{\preceq 1}$. (We will describe how to commit to _small-field_ polynomials—or that is, how to reduce the small-field case to the large-field case—[shortly](/blueprint/cryptography/switching).)

:::info
In practice, we can freely imagine $\tau = 7$; thus, $\mathcal{T}_\tau = \mathcal{T}_7$ is isomorphic as a field to $\mathbb{F}_{2^{128}}$, the binary field of order $2^{128}$. The number-of-variables parameter $\ell$ will be between 20 and 30 in practical applications.
:::

We're also going to pick a _rate_ parameter $\mathcal{R} \geq 1$, a positive integer. We can think of $\mathcal{R}$ as a "blowup factor" parameter: during the prover's commitment phase, he will "stretch" his polynomial $2^{\mathcal{R}}$-fold. Thus, if $\mathcal{R} = 1$, it will double in length; if $\mathcal{R} = 2$, it will quadruple in length, and so on. As we [hinted at](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/additive) earlier, our rate parameter $\mathcal{R}$ controls a prover–verifier tradeoff. The higher $\mathcal{R}$ gets, the costlier things get for the prover (because the code's rate gets worse), while the _cheaper_ they get for the verifier (because the code's distance gets better). The real-life selection of $\mathcal{R}$ is quite complicated (the verifier's returns on higher $\mathcal{R}$ diminish very quickly, for example).

:::info
In practice, we will basically always take $\mathcal{R} = 1$ or $\mathcal{R} = 2$.
:::

Finally, we fix a domain $S \subset \mathcal{T}_\tau$ of size $2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}}$. In fact, [as usual](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/additive#the-algorithm), we will set $S \coloneqq \left< \beta_0, \ldots , \beta_{\ell + \mathcal{R} - 1} \right>$, where $(\beta_0, \ldots , \beta_{2^\tau - 1})$ is the $\mathbb{F}_2$-basis of $\mathcal{T}_\tau$ we used to construct the novel polynomial basis.

## The Procedure

There are just a few short steps we need to do to our input $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$.

- We first take our input $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$'s coordinates in the _[multilinear Lagrange basis](/blueprint/primer)_, or in other words its evaluations $\left( t(v) \right)_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell}$ over the $\ell$-dimensional cube. (In practice, $t$ will be represented concretely this way at rest to begin with!)
- We're now do a _flattening step_. We [already know](/blueprint/primer) how to identify $\mathcal{B}_\ell$ with the flat, ordered integer range $\{0, \ldots, 2^\ell - 1\}$: we use the little-endian lexicographic identification $v \mapsto \sum_{i = 0}^{\ell - 1} 2^i \cdot v_i$. Using this identification, we flatten $t$'s coefficients into a list $(t_0, \ldots , t_{2^\ell - 1})$—indeed, we just need to set $t_{\{v\}} \coloneqq t(v)$.
- We use this flattened list as the coefficients of a _univariate_ polynomial in the [novel polynomial basis](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/additive). That is, we write $P(X) \coloneqq t_0 + t_1 \cdot X_1(X) + \cdots + t_{2^\ell - 1} \cdot X_{2^\ell - 1}(X)$.
- We then evaluate _this_ polynomial $P(X)$ over our domain $S \subset \mathcal{T}_\tau$. Using the [additive NTT](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/additive), this key step can be performed in quasilinear—that is, $\Theta(\ell \cdot 2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}})$—time. In this way, we get a long list, say $(b_0, \ldots , b_{2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}} - 1})$.
- We now need to compute a _Merkle commitment_ to this list. In cartoon terms, this looks like writing a height-$\ell + \mathcal{R}$, balanced binary tree whose leaves are $(b_0, \ldots , b_{2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}} - 1})$, and each of whose intermediate nodes is the hash of its children, and finally grabbing this tree's root. (Our real-life Merklizatzion procedure is somewhat more complicated; we refer to [[DP24](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/504), § 6.2].)

The _output_ of the commitment scheme is the Merkle root, which the prover can send directly to the verifier.
