# The Additive NTT [Binary Reed–Solomon codes, and an efficient algorithm to encode them]

The most important code we come across in SNARKs is the _[Reed–Solomon code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reed%E2%80%93Solomon_error_correction)_. This is a simple—and yet extremely powerful and effective—code, and appears not just in Binius but also in [DEEP-ALI](https://eprint.iacr.org/2019/336.pdf)-based SNARKs like [Plonky3](https://github.com/Plonky3/Plonky3) and [Stwo](https://github.com/starkware-libs/stwo).

As we've [suggested](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/codes), error-correcting codes have two desirable properties—_distance_ and _rate_—which are at odds with each other in general. Indeed, a mathematical result called the _[Singleton bound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singleton_bound#Linear_codes)_ formalizes the precise way in which these desiderata are in tension. Reed–Solomon codes _meet_ the Singleton bound, in the sense that the inequality becomes an equality; they thus negotiate the tension between these attributes as favorably as it is possible to do.

Reed–Solomon codes are not _just_ coding-theoretically nice. They also support an extremely rich set of auxiliary protocols, designed to achieve such ends as _proximity testing_ (determining whether a supplied _word_ is close to a codeword or not, by just reading from it at a few places). The crucial protocol here is _[FRI](https://eccc.weizmann.ac.il/report/2017/134/)_ (short for _fast Reed–Solomon interactive oracle proof of proximity_), due to Ben-Sasson, Bentov, Horesh and Riabzev. We will [use FRI](/blueprint/cryptography/evaluation/fri) in our evaluation protocol.

## Review of Reed–Solomon Codes

Mathematically, each Reed–Solomon code depends on a _field_ $\mathbb{F}$, a _message length_ $k$ and a _block length_ $n$. We first need to pick a _domain_ $S \subset \mathbb{F}$ of size $|S| = n$ (as a side effect, we need $| \mathbb{F} | \geq n$ for this to make sense).

Our _message space_ will be the set of univariate polynomials $P(X) = a_0 + a_1 \cdot X + \cdots + a_{k - 1} \cdot X^{k - 1}$ of degree less than $k$ with coefficients in $\mathbb{F}$. The _word space_ will be the set of all $n$-tuples of $\mathbb{F}$-elements $(b_0, \ldots , b_{n - 1})$ (here, we can think of associating these with the elements of $S$). The _codewords_ will be those $n$-tuples which arise as the set of evaluations of some polynomial $P(X)$ as above on $S$: that is, those of the form $(P(x_0), \ldots , P(x_{n - 1}))$. Here, $(x_0, \ldots , x_{n - 1})$ is an enumeration of $S$'s elements.

## The Binary Case

In Binius, Reed–Solomon codes also play a central role. On the other hand, since we work with binary fields, things get significantly trickier. The first question that arises has to do with _encoding_: the actual algorithm which takes as input a message and outputs its corresponding codeword. As should be clear from the above, this is a problem of _polynomial evaluation_: how do we take the $k$ coefficients of some polynomial, and output the $n$ values that that polynomial takes on some domain? We can trivially do this in $\Theta(k \cdot n)$ steps; the goal is to do better than that.

In the classical, prime-field setting, encoding is very standard: the key algorithm is the _[NTT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_Fourier_transform_over_a_ring#Number-theoretic_transform)_ (number-theoretic transform), a close relative of the [fast Fourier transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fast_Fourier_transform). The NTT runs in $\Theta(\log_2(k) \cdot n)$ steps. Here, the set $S \subset \mathbb{F}$ is a multiplicative subgroup $S \subset \mathbb{F}^*$ of power-of-two order $n$ (the field $\mathbb{F}$ needs to be chosen in such a way that this kind of subgroup exists).

In binary fields, primitive multiplicative roots of unity of power-of-two order don't exist, and things get significantly trickier. Indeed, it was not known until 2014—i.e., until the arrival of a key work by Lin, Chung and Han [[LCH14](https://arxiv.org/abs/1404.3458)]—how one might encode in the _binary_ setting in such a way as to recover the $\Theta(\log_2(k) \cdot n)$ time complexity of the classical algorithm.

:::note
On the other hand, [[LCH14](https://arxiv.org/abs/1404.3458)] culminated a rich line of predecessor works, including those of Cantor [[Can87](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/0097316589900204)] and Gao–Mateer [[GM10](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/5625613)]. It has also spawned important successor works, like the _Frobenius additive FFT_ of Li et al. [[Li+18](https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.03932)].
:::

Even the Lin–Chung–Han algorithm has a catch. While classical Reed–Solomon encoding algorithms understand their inputs $(a_0, \ldots , a_{k - 1})$ as the coefficients in the _standard monomial basis_ of the polynomial being evaluated, Lin, Chung and Han actually need to introduce a special polynomial basis: the so-called _novel polynomial basis_. These are specially constructed polynomials $1, X_1(X), \ldots, X_{k - 1}(X)$ _unequal_ to the simple monomials $1, X, \ldots, X^{k - 1}$. The polynomial we actually end up evaluating is _not_ $a_0 + a_1 \cdot X + \cdots + a_{k - 1} \cdot X^{k - 1}$, but rather $a_0 + a_1 \cdot X_1(X) + \cdots + a_{k - 1} \cdot X_{k - 1}(X)$.

Finally, the set $S \subset \mathbb{F}$ we evaluate _on_ is _not_ a multiplicative subgroup, but rather an _$\mathbb{F}_2$-subspace_ of $\mathbb{F}$ (recall that when the characteristic of $\mathbb{F}$ is 2, we can view it as a vector space over its own subfield $\mathbb{F}_2$).

## The Algorithm

Here, we sketch in slightly more technically precise terms what Lin, Chung and Han's algorithm [[LCH14](https://arxiv.org/abs/1404.3458)] achieves. We fix parameters $\ell$ and $\mathcal{R}$. Ultimately, the code we spin up is going to be Reed–Solomon, with message length $k = 2^\ell$ and block length $n = 2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}}$; we will write $\mathsf{RS}_{S, \mathcal{T}_\tau}[2^\ell, 2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}}]$ for this code. We fix a _binary tower_ field $\mathcal{T}_\tau$, and write $\beta_0, \ldots , \beta_{2^\tau - 1}$ for an $\mathbb{F}_2$-basis of it. From this $\mathbb{F}_2$-basis of $\mathcal{T}_\tau$, [[LCH14](https://arxiv.org/abs/1404.3458)] tells us how to construct a further _basis of polynomials_—called the _novel polynomial basis_—which we will write as $X_0(X), \ldots, X_{2^\ell - 1}(X)$. Each $X_i(X)$ will be a polynomial over $\mathcal{T}_\tau$ of degree $i$. In fact, these polynomials will themselves constitute a $\mathcal{T}_\tau$-basis of the $2^\ell$-dimensional vector space $\mathcal{T}_\tau[X]^{\prec 2^\ell}$ (i.e., of the set of polynomials over $\mathcal{T}_\tau$ of degree less than $2^\ell$).

Finally, we write $S \subset \mathcal{T}_\tau$ for the $\mathbb{F}_2$-span of the basis prefix $\beta_0, \ldots , \beta_{\ell + \mathcal{R} - 1}$.

The additive NTT is an algorithm that takes as input a list $(a_0, \ldots , a_{2^\ell - 1})$ and returns a list $(b_0, \ldots , b_{2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}} - 1})$ defined in the following way. We write $P(X) = a_0 + a_1 \cdot X_1(X) + \cdots + X_{2^\ell - 1}(X)$ for the polynomial whose coefficients in the _novel basis_ are the given input $(a_0, \ldots , a_{2^\ell - 1})$. What we return is the list $(P(x_0), \ldots , P(x_{2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}} - 1}))$ of $P$'s evaluations on $S$. Here, $(x_0, \ldots , x_{2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}} - 1})$ is again an enumeration of $S$. In practice, we will get this by taking the lexicographically ordered basis combinations of $\beta_0, \ldots , \beta_{\ell + \mathcal{R} - 1}$.

We refrain here from describing _how the additive NTT actually works_ (i.e., how it achieves the above task). In short, Lin, Chung and Han [[LCH14](https://arxiv.org/abs/1404.3458)] rely on a totally new sort of polynomial decomposition, specific to the _novel basis polynomials_. In [[DP24](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/504), § 3], we show that Lin–Chung–Han's NTT is can actually be _re-characterized_ in terms more familiarly Fourier-theoretic.
