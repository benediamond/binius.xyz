# Review of FRI [A crash course on the classic proximity test]

As we've [already mentioned](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/additive), our commitment scheme uses _Reed–Solomon codes_, and particular binary ones. Here, we will begin to sketch _why_ our use of codes pays off. The crucial underlying mathematical tool turns out to be _[FRI](https://eccc.weizmann.ac.il/report/2017/134/)_—short for _fast Reed–Solomon interactive oracle proof of proximity_—a key protocol developed by Ben-Sasson, Bentov, Horesh and Riabzev.

FRI is an extremely complicated topic, and we will simply not be able to cover all of its ins and outs here. In short, FRI is a _proximity test_—an interactive protocol, between a prover and a verifier—pertaining to a committed _word_. The verifier's goal is to determine whether the word is _close to a codeword_—that is, whether it differs from the codeword at just a few positions, or at many; in the process, the verifier would like to query as few positions of the committed word as possible. A key aspect of the protocol is _interaction_: during the protocol, the prover and verifier interact over the course of a logarithmic number of rounds (in the length of the initial committed word). In each successive round, the prover commits to a _further_ word, and the verifier samples and sends a random challenge. In the end, the verifier runs a testing procedure known as _querying_.

Here, we describe FRI in the _binary setting_—the setting, in fact, in which most of that protocol's [original treatment](https://eccc.weizmann.ac.il/report/2017/134/) was carried out. On the other hand, we moreover _specialize_ binary FRI in a key way, itself introduced in [[DP24](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/504), § 3.1]. Our specialization is designed to make FRI compatible with the [additive NTT](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/additive).
