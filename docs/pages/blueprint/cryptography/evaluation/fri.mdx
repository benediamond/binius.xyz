# Review of FRI [A crash course on the classic proximity test]

As we've [already mentioned](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/additive), our commitment scheme uses _Reed–Solomon codes_, and particular binary ones. Here, we will begin to sketch _why_ our use of codes pays off. The crucial underlying mathematical tool turns out to be _FRI_—short for _fast Reed–Solomon interactive oracle proof of proximity_—a key protocol developed by Ben-Sasson, Bentov, Horesh and Riabzev [[BBHR18](https://eccc.weizmann.ac.il/report/2017/134/)].

FRI is an extremely complicated topic, and we will simply not be able to cover all of its ins and outs here. In short, FRI is a _proximity test_—an interactive protocol, between a prover and a verifier—pertaining to a committed _word_. The verifier's goal is to determine whether the word is _close to a codeword_—that is, whether it differs from _some_ (i.e., any) codeword at just a few positions, or whether it's instead far from them all. In the process, the verifier would like to query as few positions of the committed word as possible. FRI resides firmly in the [IOP model](/blueprint/background/models#the-iop-model). A key aspect of the protocol is _interaction_: during the protocol, the prover and verifier interact over the course of a logarithmic number of rounds (in the length of the initial committed word). In each round, the verifier samples and sends a random challenge, and the prover commits a _further_ word to the string oracle. In the end, the verifier runs a testing procedure known as _querying_, during which it reads the prover's various committed words at various points.

We conduct our overview of FRI in the _binary setting_—the setting, in fact, in which most of that protocol's [original treatment](https://eccc.weizmann.ac.il/report/2017/134/) was carried out. On the other hand, we moreover specialize binary-field FRI _itself_ in a key way, following [[DP24](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/504), § 3.1]. Our specialization is designed to make FRI compatible with the [additive NTT](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/additive).

## Some Mathematical Objects

During our description of the [additive NTT](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/additive), we worked with a binary field $\mathcal{T}_\tau$, a size parameters $\ell$, and a rate parameter $\mathcal{R}$. Our encoding algorithm, on input a list $(a_0, \ldots , a_{2^{\ell - 1}})$, gave us a further list $(b_0, \ldots , b_{2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}} - 1})$, which arose as the _evaluations_ of a certain polynomial $P(X)$ of degree less than $2^\ell$ on $S$; here, $S \subset \mathcal{T}_\tau$ is a domain of dimension $\ell + \mathcal{R}$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$. In fact, we chose $S$ to be an $\mathbb{F}_2$-vector subspace of $\mathcal{T}_\tau$ (specifically, the span of the basis prefix $\left< \beta_0, \ldots , \beta_{\ell + \mathcal{R} - 1} \right>$). Our [commitment procedure](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/procedure) ultimately wrapped up by sending $(b_0, \ldots , b_{2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}}})$ to the string oracle.

In FRI, the domain $S^{(0)} \coloneqq S$ becomes just the first in a _sequence_ of domains $S^{(0)}, S^{(1)}, \ldots , S^{(\ell)}$. Each of these shrinks by exactly one dimension; in particular, $S^{(1)}$ is $\ell + \mathcal{R} - 1$-dimensional, and $S^{(\ell)}$ is just $\mathcal{R}$-dimensional as an $\mathbb{F}_2$-vector subspace of $\mathcal{T}_\tau$. In fact, we link up these domains using a series of $\mathbb{F}_2$-linear maps:

$$
\begin{equation*}S^{(0)} \xrightarrow{q^{(0)}} S^{(1)} \xrightarrow{q^{(1)}} S^{(2)} \xrightarrow{q^{(2)}} \cdots \xrightarrow{q^{(\ell - 1)}} S^{(\ell)}.\end{equation*}
$$

Here, each map $q^{(i)} : S^{(i)} \to S^{(i + 1)}$ is linear, with a _one-dimensional_ kernel. In particular, $q^{(i)}$ vanishes on a 1-dimensional subspace of $S^{(i)}$; moreover, the image of its restriction to $S^{(i)}$ is exactly $S^{(i + 1)}$, a subspace whose dimension is exactly one less than $S^{(i)}$'s.

Finally, the committed word $(b_0, \ldots , b_{2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}} - 1})$, which we view as a function $f^{(0)} : S^{(0)} \to \mathcal{T}_\tau$, becomes just _the first_ in a series of smaller and smaller words $f^{(1)}, \ldots , f^{(\ell - 1)}$ committed to by the prover. Each committed word $f^{(i)}$ is a function on $S^{(i)}$. To describe these further words, we need to describe the structure of the FRI protocol.

## Rough Idea of FRI

The FRI protocol proceeds over $\ell$ rounds. We start with the prover's initial committed word $f^{(0)} : S^{(0)} \to \mathcal{T}_\tau$. For each round index $i \in \{0, \ldots , \ell - 1\}$,

- The verifier samples a random scalar $r_i \gets \mathcal{T}_\tau$, and sends it to the prover.
- The prover, using its existing oracle $f^{(i)} : S^{(i)} \to \mathcal{T}_\tau$ and $r_i$, carries out a procedure called "FRI folding", which depends on both $f^{(i)}$ _and_ $r_i$. Using this procedure, the prover comes up with a _further_ word $f^{(i + 1)} : S^{(i + 1)} \to \mathcal{T}_\tau$. It commits this word to the oracle.

The exception is the very final round, the $\ell$th. In this round, instead of committing to $f^{(\ell)}$, the prover just sends a single constant to the verifier. Finally, the verifier kicks off its _querying phase_. At the end of this phase, the verifier either _accepts_ or _rejects_.

The key idea necessary to understand FRI is _closeness to the Reed–Solomon code_. If the prover is honest, then, for each $i \in \{0, \ldots , \ell - 1\}$, the committed word $f^{(i)}$ will be a _codeword_ in the Reed–Solomon code $\mathsf{RS}_{S^{(i)}, \mathcal{T}_\tau}[2^{\ell - i}, 2^{\ell + \mathcal{R} - i}]$. That is, it will arise as the _evaluations_ of some polynomial of degree less than $2^{\ell - i}$ on the size-$2^{\ell + \mathcal{R} - i}$ domain $S^{(\ell - i)}$.

On the other hand, if the prover is dishonest, then its oracles might not be codewords; they might even be _far_ from the code, in the sense that they don't even reside _near any_ codeword, forget about equalling one. Here, the notion "far from the code" is fairly tricky. Roughly, we can say that $f^{(0)}$ is _far_ from $C$ if $d(f^{(0)}, C) \geq \frac{d}{2}$, in the sense that, for _each_ codeword $\overline{f}{}^{(0)} \in C$, $d(f^{(0)}, \overline{f}{}^{(0)}) \geq \frac{d}{2}$ holds. Here, $d = n - k + 1 = 2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}} - 2^\ell + 1$ is the _distance_ of the Reed–Solomon code $\mathsf{RS}_{S, \mathcal{T}_\tau}[2^\ell, 2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}}]$. The right-hand side $\frac{d}{2}$ represents $C$'s _unique decoding radius_. If $f^{(0)}$'s distance from $C$ is _less than_ $\frac{d}{2}$, then there exists a codeword $\overline{f}{}^{(0)} \in C$ such that $d(f^{(0)}, \overline{f}{}^{(0)}) < \frac{d}{2}$ holds, and moreover $\overline{f}{}^{(0)}$ is unique. Otherwise, the codeword $\overline{f}{}^{(0)}$ won't exist.

FRI's security guarantee entails that if the prover's _initial_ word $f^{(0)} : S^{(0)} \to \mathcal{T}_\tau$ is far from the initial code $C := \mathsf{RS}_{S, \mathcal{T}_\tau}[2^\ell, 2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}}]$, then the verifier will reject during its querying phase with overwhelming probability. This is true regardless of how the prover constructs its positive-indexed oracles $f^{(1)}, \ldots , f^{(\ell - 1)}$.

Thus, stepping back, we see why FRI is called a _proximity test_: its overall purpose is to detect whether some committed word $f^{(0)} : S^{(0)} \to \mathcal{T}_\tau$ is _close_ or not. If the thing is not close, then the verifier will reject.

## The NTT-Friendly Specialization

As we've mentioned, we use the [additive NTT](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/additive) to encode our initial Reed–Solomon codeword $f^{(0)} : S^{(0)} \to \mathcal{T}_\tau$. As it happens, we are going to moreover want to choose the FRI folding maps $q^{(0)} : S^{(0)} \to \mathcal{T}_\tau$ in a _particular_ way, in order to make those maps work "compatibly" with the additive NTT.

The particular sort of "compatibility" we're after here is pretty complicated, and we are going to essentially punt to [[DP24](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/504), § 3.1] for the full treatment. Here, we give an extremely rough sketch.

We've already mentioned that, _if_ the prover is honest, then each word $f^{(i)} : S^{(i)} \to \mathcal{T}_\tau$ will be a codeword in $\mathsf{RS}_{S, \mathcal{T}_\tau}[2^\ell, 2^{\ell + \mathcal{R}}]$—that is, an evaluation of some polynomial $P^{(i)}(X)$ of degree less than $2^{\ell - i}$ on $S^{(i)}$. But _which polynomial_? In the case of $i = 0$, we already know, by definition of our [commitment scheme](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/procedure): that is, it will be nothing other than

$$
\begin{equation*}P^{(0)}(X) \coloneqq a_0 + a_1 \cdot X_1(X) + \cdots + a_{2^\ell - 1} \cdot X_{2^\ell - 1}(X),\end{equation*}
$$

where $(a_0, \ldots , a_{2^\ell - 1})$ are the (flattened) coefficients of our initial multilinear. This is simply the definition of our commitment scheme. What about for positive $i > 0$?

In the work BaseFold [[ZCF23](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1705.pdf)], it is noted that _in the prime-field setting_, FRI folding acts in a "predictable" way upon the coefficients of the polynomial $P^{(i)}(X)$ underlying $f^{(i)}(X)$. That is, if $P^{(i)}(X)$'s coefficients are

$$
\begin{equation*}(a^{(i)}_0, \ldots , a^{(i)}_{2^{\ell - i} - 1}),\end{equation*}
$$

let's say, then $f^{(i + 1)} = \mathsf{fold}(f^{(i)}, r_i)$ will be the evaluation on $S^{(i + 1)}$ of the polynomial $P^{(i + 1)}(X)$ with coefficients

$$
\begin{equation*}(a^{(i)}_0 + r_i \cdot a^{(i)}_1, \ldots , a^{(i)}_{2^{\ell - i} - 2} + r_i \cdot a^{(i)}_{2^{\ell - i} - 1}).\end{equation*}
$$

That is, _in the coefficient domain_, FRI folding amounts to an even–odd folding of adjacent pairs of elements, using the random challenge $r_i$.

In [[DP24](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/504), § 3.1], we describe a _particular_ construction of the maps $q^{(i)} : S^{(i)} \to S^{(i + 1)}$ which causes this behavior to re-emerge in the binary setting. We emphasize that for maps $q^{(i)} : S^{(i)} \to S^{(i + 1)}$ chosen arbitrarily—and [[BBHR18](https://eccc.weizmann.ac.il/report/2017/134/)] does _not_ suggest a particular choice—the required even–odd folding behavior will not be preserved.
