# The Procedure [Expressing small-field claims using tensors and sums]

Here, without further ado, we describe the steps of ring-switching. Here, we have an $\ell$-variate polynomial $t(X_0, \ldots, X_{\ell - 1})$ fixed and [committed to](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment); we are going to give a protocol to evaluate its $\kappa$-fold _[refinement](/blueprint/cryptography/switching/refinement)_ $t'(X_0, \ldots, X_{\ell + \kappa - 1})$—specifically, to process an _evaluation claim_ of the form $s \stackrel{?}= t'(r_0, \ldots, r_{\ell + \kappa - 1})$.

Our goal will be to reduce that initial evaluation claim to a sum claim on $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$—i.e., of the form $s_0 \stackrel{?}= \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} t(v) \cdot A(v)$, where $A(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ is some further, transparent, multilinear.

## The Steps

Here is the algorithm.

- By embedding the original (packed) multilinear $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ componentwise _horizontally_ into $A$, we can express it as an $\ell$-variate multilinear over $A$—$\varphi_1(t)$, let's say. The prover computes and sends to the verifier

  $$
  \begin{equation*}\hat{s} \coloneqq \varphi_1(t)(\varphi_0(r_\kappa), \ldots, \varphi_0(r_{\ell + \kappa - 1})).\end{equation*}
  $$
  - The element $\hat{s}$ is an _algebra element_, since the entire evaluation above takes place in the algebra. Thus, concretely, $\hat{s}$ looks like a $2^\kappa \times 2^\kappa$ array of $\mathcal{T}_\iota$-elements.
  - It is a bit tricky, but a very important and doable exercise, to study concretely what $\hat{s}$ looks like. In fact, its _columns_ $\left( \hat{s}_v \right)_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\kappa}$ will be exactly the _partial evaluations_ $\hat{s}_v = t'(v_0, \ldots, v_{\kappa - 1}, r_\kappa, \ldots , r_{\ell + \kappa - 1})$. Roughly, this is because the vertically embedded elements $\varphi_0(r_\kappa), \ldots, \varphi_0(r_{\ell + \kappa - 1})$ will act "slice-wise" on the cells of the coefficients $\varphi_1(t)(w)$, for $w \in \mathcal{B}_\ell$ (recall that these coefficients are _horizontally_ embedded). We prove this fact rigorously in our paper (see [[DP24](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/504), Thm. 4.2]).
- Writing $\left( \hat{s}_v \right)_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\kappa}$ for $\hat{s}$'s columns, the verifier checks:

  $$
  \begin{equation*}s \stackrel{?}= \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\kappa} \hat{s}_v \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r_0, \ldots, r_{\kappa - 1}, v_0, \ldots , v_{\kappa - 1}).\end{equation*}
  $$

  - By the discussion just above, we can immediately see why this check is complete. Indeed, taking on faith for now that $\hat{s}_v = t'(v_0, \ldots, v_{\kappa - 1}, r_\kappa, \ldots , r_{\ell + \kappa - 1})$ will hold for each $v \in \mathcal{B}_\kappa$ if the prover is honest, we thus see that the right-hand side above will equal $\sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\kappa} t'(v_0, \ldots, v_{\kappa - 1}, r_\kappa, \ldots , r_{\ell + \kappa - 1}) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r_0, \ldots, r_{\kappa - 1}, v_0, \ldots , v_{\kappa - 1}) = t'(r_0, \ldots , r_{\ell + \kappa - 1})$; this quantity in turn equals $s$ precisely when the prover's initial evaluation claim is true.
- The verifier samples row-batching scalars $(r''_0, \ldots , r''_{\kappa - 1}) \gets \mathcal{T}_\tau^\kappa$. The verifier ships these off to the prover. Meanwhile, locally, it decomposes $\hat{s}$ into _rows_ $\left( \hat{s}_u \right)_{u \in \mathcal{B}_\kappa}$, and computes the row-combination:

  $$
  \begin{equation*}s_0 \coloneqq \sum_{u \in \mathcal{B}_\kappa} \hat{s}_u \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r''_0, \ldots , r''_{\kappa - 1}, u_0, \ldots , u_{\kappa - 1}).\end{equation*}
  $$

  In parallel, the verifier conjures into its head an odd sort of multilinear $A(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$, defined in the following way. For each $w \in \mathcal{B}_\ell$, we have a basis-decomposition:

  $$
  \begin{equation*}\widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r_\kappa, \ldots , r_{\ell + \kappa - 1}, w_0, \ldots , w_{\ell - 1}) = \sum_{u \in \mathcal{B}_\kappa} A_{w, u} \cdot \beta_u.\end{equation*}
  $$

  I.e., whatever the left-hand $\mathcal{T}_\tau$-element above happens to be, surely it has a coordinate decomposition with respect to the usual $\mathcal{T}_\iota$-basis of $\mathcal{T}_\tau$; that coordinate decomposition is what appears on the right. Finally, the verifier _defines_ $A(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ to be the multilinear extension of the function $A : \mathcal{B}_\ell \to \mathcal{T}_\tau$ that sends:

  $$
  \begin{equation*}A : w \mapsto \sum_{u \in \mathcal{B}_\kappa} A_{w, u} \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r''_0, \ldots , r''_{\kappa - 1}, u_0, \ldots , u_{\kappa - 1}).\end{equation*}
  $$
    - If we work extremely concretely, we can see clearly what $A(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ is. Essentially, there are two steps. First, we take the tensor-expansion $\left( \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(w_0, \ldots, w_{\ell - 1}, r_\kappa, \ldots , r_{\ell + \kappa - 1}) \right)_{w \in \mathcal{B}_\ell}$. This looks like a long, length-$2^\ell$ vector of $\mathcal{T}_\tau$-elements; we can imagine this thing going horizontally. By decomposing _each_ component of this vector along the standard $\mathcal{T}_\iota$-basis of $\mathcal{T}_\tau$, we can rather express this thing as a $2^\kappa \times 2^\ell$ _matrix_ with entries in $\mathcal{T}_\iota$. By _row-combining_ this $\mathcal{T}_\iota$-matrix using the length-$2^\kappa$ tensor expansion $\left( \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r''_0, \ldots , r''_{\kappa - 1}, u_0, \ldots , u_{\kappa - 1}) \right)_{u \in \mathcal{B}_\kappa}$, we finally get one last long, horizontal vector of $\mathcal{T}_\tau$-elements, again of length $2^\ell$. This vector is exactly the vector of values that $A(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ takes on the cube.
    - This might sound crazy, but the verifier isn't _doing_ any of these computations. Indeed, that wouldn't be succinct at all! Rather, the verifier is simply _defining_ this polynomial in its head right now, not evaluating it anywhere yet (let alone on the entire cube).
    - Indeed, as it turns out, the verifier _will_ ultimately evaluate $A(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ at exactly one point—that point will show up during the processing of the sum claim we're about to output. Details are [here](/blueprint/cryptography/evaluation).
- We're done! The verifier now outputs the _sum claim_
  $$
  \begin{equation*}s_0 \stackrel{?}= \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} t(v) \cdot A(v).\end{equation*}
  $$
    - Above, we agreed that we wanted to output a sum claim of the above form for which $A(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ is _transparent_. Indeed, it's a far-from-obvious fact—which we prove in our paper—[[DP24](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/504)]—that $A(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ _is transparent_. Indeed, the verifier can evaluate it at any point $(r'_0, \ldots , r'_{\ell - 1})$ by just doing $O(\ell)$ $\mathcal{T}_\tau$-multiplications.
    - This latter fact is where the tensor algebra really shines. Indeed, the verifier's succinct evaluation procedure for $A(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ will involve the tensor algebra's multiplicative structure in a crucial way. In fact, it appears essential: we _don't know_ how to write down a succinct evaluation procedure for $A(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ that _doesn't_ use the the tensor algebra!
    - For now, we defer the proof that $A(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ is transparent, referring to our paper.

## Some Intuitions and Remarks

In a rigorous treatment, there are a few things we are going to have to establish to even show that this thing is complete, let alone secure. And for the _sum claim_ we just output at the end just now to make sense, $A(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ should of course be transparent—a fact which we're again going to punt on.

For now, though, we address a key fact: why the sum claim $s_0 \stackrel{?}= \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} t(v) \cdot A(v)$ _is even true_ (i.e., assuming the prover is honest)?

### An algebra-valued sumcheck

Here is the rough idea. Back in its very first message, the prover was supposed to compute $\hat{s} \coloneqq \varphi_1(t)(\varphi_0(r_\kappa), \ldots, \varphi_0(r_{\ell + \kappa - 1}))$. By multilinearly expanding this thing's right-hand side, we get an identity of $A$-elements, which _should_ hold if the prover is honest:

$$
\begin{equation*}\hat{s} \stackrel{?}= \sum_{w \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} \varphi_1(t)(w) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(\varphi_0(r_\kappa), \ldots , \varphi_0(r_{\ell + \kappa - 1}), w_0, \ldots , w_{\ell - 1}).\end{equation*}
$$

Intuitively, what we would _like_ to do is sumcheck this thing. The sumcheck would be over _$A$-valued_ multilinears. Our challenges $(r'_0, \ldots , r'_{\ell - 1})$ would come from the usual field $\mathcal{T}_\tau$; before using these, though, we will implicitly embed them _horizontally_ (i.e., we will really use $(\varphi_1(r'_0), \ldots , \varphi_1(r'_{\ell - 1}))$). Thus, it's an odd kind of sumcheck, where the polynomials are over $A$, but the challenges come from the subring $\varphi_1(\mathcal{T}_\tau) \subset A$. It turns out that this sumcheck variant is secure.

### Reducing to an evaluation

What's the point? If we can do this sumcheck, we're in great shape, since, as of its very end, we will have reduced the sum claim to an _evaluation claim_ at the challenge point $(\varphi_1(r'_0), \ldots , \varphi_1(r'_{\ell - 1}))$: namely, to learning

$$
\begin{equation*}\varphi_1(t)(\varphi_1(r'_0), \ldots , \varphi_1(r'_{\ell - 1})) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(\varphi_0(r_\kappa), \ldots , \varphi_0(r_{\ell + \kappa - 1}), \varphi_1(r'_0), \ldots , \varphi_1(r'_{\ell - 1})).\end{equation*}
$$

The right-hand factor above is succinct: the verifier can compute it by doing $O(\ell)$ operations in $A$ (in fact, $2 \cdot \ell$ $\mathcal{T}_\tau$-operations are enough, as we argue in our paper [[DP24](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/504.pdf), Rem. 4.4]). The left-hand factor, on the other hand, is $\varphi_1(t)(\varphi_1(r'_0), \ldots , \varphi_1(r'_{\ell - 1})) = \varphi_1\left( t(r'_0, \ldots , r'_{\ell - 1}) \right)$. But this is just the same as learning $t(r'_0, \ldots , r'_{\ell - 1})$! We thus successfully reduced to an evaluation of $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$, which is what we wanted.

### Row-combination

Now, for _purely efficiency reasons_, we can get some gains by batching the rows of the above sumcheck, and running it over $\mathcal{T}_\tau$, as opposed to over $A$ itself. It turns out that this batching process is secure, for standard reasons. So, the sum claim we _actually_ output in the ring-switching reduction above is nothing other than that which we obtain from the $A$-valued sum claim $\hat{s} \stackrel{?}= \sum_{w \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} \varphi_1(t)(w) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(\varphi_0(r_\kappa), \ldots , \varphi_0(r_{\ell + \kappa - 1}), w_0, \ldots , w_{\ell - 1})$ above upon batching that claim's rows (i.e., using the batching scalars $(r''_0, \ldots , r''_{\kappa - 1})$). As for the left-hand side, we saw already that $s_0 \coloneqq \sum_{u \in \mathcal{B}_\kappa} \hat{s}_u \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r''_0, \ldots , r''_{\kappa - 1}, u_0, \ldots , u_{\kappa - 1})$, so that's not hard. The trickier claim is that the multilinear $A(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ we defined above comes from _row-batching_ the $A$-valued multilinear $\widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(\varphi_0(r_\kappa), \ldots , \varphi_0(r_{\ell + \kappa - 1}), X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$. But even this fact becomes relatively explainable if you stare long enough at how the multilinear $A(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ is constructed.

## Efficiency

If you stare at what's written above, you'll see that the amount of work for the verifier is actually _very little_. In fact, the verifier has only to receive the $A$-element $\hat{s}$, which takes $2^\iota \cdot 2^{2 \cdot \kappa} = 2^\tau \cdot 2^\kappa$ bits to represent (a few kilobytes in the worst case). Moreover, the verifier has to compute _two different_ combinations of $\hat{s}$: a column-combination and a row-combination. Both combination vectors are _tensors_ (i.e., of $(r_0, \ldots , r_{\kappa - 1})$ and $(r''_0, \ldots , r''_{\kappa - 1})$, respectively). Each of these tensors takes $\Theta(2^\kappa)$ work to compute; the combinations themselves each amount to $\Theta(2^\kappa)$ multiplications in the tower field $\mathcal{T}_\tau$.

:::info
For example, taking $\tau = 7$, $\kappa = 4$, and $\iota = 7 - 4 = 3$, we see that $\hat{s}$ is a $16 \times 16$ array, each of whose elements is a byte. Its total size is thus just 256 bytes. The verifier's column-combinations and row-combinations each amount to 16 multiplications in the 128-bit tower field $\mathcal{T}_\tau$.

In the case $\tau = 7$, $\kappa = 7$ and $\iota = 0$—the size for which $\hat{s}$ becomes _largest_—$\hat{s}$'s size becomes just over 2 kilobytes.
:::

## Batching

We've already discussed [batching](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/batched) in the context of our commitment scheme. Ring-switching has its own batching story, which is quite complicated. As a conceptual starting point, we can imagine a non-batched version, in which the parties carry out the above procedure _independently_ for each refined evaluation claim at issue (the prover sends _all_ resulting tensor algebra elements to the verifier). Upon processing each of these tensor algebra elements independently, the verifier would thus output _many_ sum claims (all pertaining to huge-field polynomials of different lengths).

Even in this non-batched version, our [next step](/blueprint/cryptography/evaluation) will certainly be batched. That is, provided that underlying polynomials $t_0, \ldots , t_{n - 1}$ were committed to using our batched commitment [procedure](/blueprint/cryptography/commitment/batched), we will have [a way](/blueprint/cryptography/evaluation) to process _all_ of the resulting sum claims in one go.

With this said, in practice we want to carry out a further sort of batching at the ring-switching level, whereby fewer tensor-algebra elements have to get sent. We have a batching scheme which works in the following rough way. We fix a huge amount $n$ (say, thousands) of claims. Each looks like an $\ell_i$-variate polynomial $t_i(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell_i - 1})$ over $\mathcal{T}_\tau$, a refinement factor $\kappa_i$, and an evaluation claim $t'_i(r_{i, 0}, \ldots , r_{i, \ell_i + \kappa_i - 1}) \stackrel{?}= s_i$. We first coalesce these claims into "groups", by collecting into a single group all of those claims which share a common refinement factor $\kappa_i$ _and_ a common prefix $(r_{i, 0}, \ldots , r_{i, \kappa_i - 1})$. That is, we group together evaluation claims for which the refinement factor $\kappa$ is the same _and_ the first $\kappa$ components of the evaluation point are the same. We write $m$ for the resulting number of _groups_ (i.e., the number of distinct refinement-factor–prefix pairs exhibited by the claims list). Our batching scheme lets us send to the verifier just $m$ tensor-algebra elements, though it entails sending $n$ _field elements_. This ends up being efficient enough for our purposes.
