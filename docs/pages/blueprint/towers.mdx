# Towers of Binary Fields [A crash course on our basic datatypes]

Binius uses key mathematical objects called _towers of binary fields_. These are integral to everything we do. In this page, we explain them, _starting from the user's perspective_. Indeed, towers appear _both_ in our [frontend](/blueprint/arithmetization) _and_ in our [backend](/blueprint/reductions). Leaving aside mathematics and cryptography, then, even _users_ of Binius need to understand just a tiny bit about them.

## The Datatypes

In Binius, we introduce a new [arithmetization](/arithmetization) scheme, called _M3_ (short for _multi-multiset matching_). Before getting into that, we sketch here just as much about towers as a _user_ of our scheme needs to know.

In short, our fundamental datatypes are bitstrings of power-of-two width. Thus, we allow 128-bit binary strings, 64-bit strings, 32-bit strings, and so on, down to 8-bit bytes. We also allow 1-bit values. (For technical reasons, we skip the 4-bit and 2-bit widths.)

We also need to be able to talk about _adding_ and _multiplying_ strings like these. For us, addition is just bitwise XOR. Multiplication is more complicated; we refrain from explaining it for now.

Throughout, we will use symbols like $\mathcal{T}_\iota$ to refer to these datatypes. For now, $\mathcal{T}_\iota$ means "the datatype consisting of binary strings of width $2^\iota$".

:::info
For example, $\mathcal{T}_7$ is the datatype consisting of 128-bit binary strings. We will often refer to this as the "cryptographically large field" or the "huge field". $\mathcal{T}_0$ is the datatype consisting of 1-bit values (bits). $\mathcal{T}_3$ is the datatype of bytes.
:::

:::note
We note that our binary datatypes are arguably _simpler_ than those associated with prime fields. For example, [RISC Zero](https://risczero.com/)'s arithmetization uses the _Baby Bear_ prime field $\mathbb{F}_p$, where $p = 2^{31} - 2^{27} + 1$. Concretely, element of $\mathbb{F}_p$ look like integers in the range $\{0, \ldots , p - 1\}$.

These integers take _almost_ 32 bits to represent—but not quite. Indeed, at the level of bits, these integers look essentially like 32-bit binary strings—with the caveat that integers in the upper range $\{p, \ldots , 2^{32} - 1\}$ are not allowed. Elements in this range are called "non-canonical", since by subtracting off some multiples of $p$, we can replace them with elements of the canonical range $\{0, \ldots , p - 1\}$.

In Binius, $\mathcal{T}_\iota$'s elements map _exactly_ to the set of length-$2^\iota$ bitstrings, in a perfect one-to-one correspondence. There are no "forbidden" or "non-canonical" bit-strings.
:::

## The Algebra

Under the hood, our power-of-two-width binary strings actually represent elements of algebraic objects called _binary towers_. We emphasize that the algebraic details underneath these objects are _not_ necessary for consumers of Binius—those interested in writing circuits and programs. On the other hand, they will prove necessary throughout our explanation of our backend.
