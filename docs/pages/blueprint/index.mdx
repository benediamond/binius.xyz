# Blueprint [Cryptographic details about our backend]

In this site section, we explain the cryptographic backend of Binius64.

## Site Directory

We've split this site area into a few subsections.

<Callout type="note">
<Collapsible>
[**Math Background.**](math) This section contains a crash course on the mathematical objects we use.

Start here if you're new. We can't promise to cover everything, but it'll be a useful starting point.
</Collapsible>
</Callout>

<Callout type="note">
<Collapsible>
[**Constraint Systems.**](constraints) _Constraint systems_ are how we specify the computations we set about proving and verifying. Think somewhere in between "circuits" and "R1CS".

Binius64 introduces a new constraint system. Our constraint system basically takes R1CS, makes it operate fundamentally on 64-bit unsigned integers, and moreover enriches it by natively supporting constraints that act upon (logical, arithmetic, etc.) _bitshifts_ of this witness data. We also bake in support for unsigned, 64-bit integer multiplication.

The constraint system we get out is predictable and simple, but much more useful and efficient than plain, vanilla R1CS in characteristic 2.
</Collapsible>
</Callout>

<Callout type="note">
<Collapsible>
[**Oblong Multilinears.**](oblong) Our first cryptographic step is sumcheck-like. Exploiting our 64-bit word structure, we can use Gruen's [[Gru24](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/108)]'s _univariate skip_ idea in a really elegant way.

To explain how our technique works, we first need to review _oblong-multilinearization_, a technique that comes from Gruen. We also explain a few implementation-oriented considerations. The goal of this step is to reduce the overall satisfaction of AND constraints to _evaluation claims_ on the oblong-multilinearizations of certain polynomials.
</Collapsible>
</Callout>

<Callout type="note">
<Collapsible>
[**The Shift Reduction.**](shifts) This is arguably the most technically involved step in our backend. Here, we reduce evaluation claims on our oblong interpolants directly to an evaluation claim on the witness.

Internally, this step involves a multi-stage sumcheck—very roughly like Spartan [[Set20](https://eprint.iacr.org/2019/550.pdf)]'s, but much more complicated. First off, we need to deal with shifting; to do this, we adapt a bunch of ideas from [[DP25](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1784)].

We also sketch how we implement our sumchecks efficiently.
</Collapsible>
</Callout>

<Callout type="note">
<Collapsible>
[**Integer Multiplication.**](multiplication) Beyond bitwise AND constraints, our constraint system separately lets us carry out unsigned, 64-bit integer multiplication.

To handle these, we use a trick based on GKR, and on working "in the exponent".
</Collapsible>
</Callout>

<Callout type="note">
<Collapsible>
[**Polynomial Commitment.**](commitment) The "lowermost" layer of our cryptographic stack deals with the assessment of evaluation claims about multilinear polynomials.

We don't say a ton here. Mostly, we refer out to [[DP24](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/504)], which describes in detail the polynomial commitment scheme that we use.
</Collapsible>
</Callout>
