Binius's _backend_ spans roughly two layers, which we outline now.

<Callout type="note">
<Collapsible>
[**The Reductions.**](reductions) This layer contains a plurality of reduction techniques, aimed largely at progressively _simplifying_ and _unifying_ claims output by the arithmetization layer.

Indeed, our arithmetization layer computational problems into [mathematical claims](/blueprint/mathematizing), themselves either _multiset equality_ claims or _vanishing_ claims (these sorts of claims respectively correspond to the balancing of channels and to the satisfaction of gate constraints).

Our _reduction layer_ serves to progressively unroll, simplify, and unify these sorts of cryptographic claims. More specifically, the layer's goal is to reduce these cryptographic claims—which amount to complex predicates on _committed_ and _virtual_ polynomials—to simple _evaluation claims_. An _evaluation claim_, by definition, entails that a particular committed multilinear, at a particular point, evaluates to a particular value. These are the sorts of claims that are amenable to direct evaluation by our PCS.

Our cryptographic reduction layer is described in detail [here](/blueprint/reductions).
</Collapsible>
</Callout>

<Callout type="note">
<Collapsible>
[**The Cryptographic Layer.**](cryptography) Here reside the cryptographic protocols and algorithms which make it possible to securely commit to—and later, to evaluate—multilinear polynomials over tiny fields.

In this site section, we sketch in plain language Binius's approach to these complex cryptographic tasks—which itself implements and extends the academic work [[DP24](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1784)]. Indeed, we summarize that work at a more-informal level of detail, and moreover discuss the various practice-oriented extensions and generalizations of that work implemented in Binius.
</Collapsible>
</Callout>
