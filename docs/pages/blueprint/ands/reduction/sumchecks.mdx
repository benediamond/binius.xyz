# The Sumchecks [The two phases of the shift reduction]

Here, we get into the details.

We jump right into it. We recall that at [the end](/blueprint/ands/oblong/univariate#the-end) of the univariate skip, we left off with three evaluation claims made by the prover:

$$
\begin{align*}
\alpha_a &\stackrel{?}= \widehat{a}(r'_{\widehat{i}},  r'_x), \\
\alpha_b &\stackrel{?}= \widehat{b}(r'_{\widehat{i}},  r'_x), \\
\alpha_c &\stackrel{?}= \widehat{c}(r'_{\widehat{i}},  r'_x),
\end{align*}
$$

As of this point, the verifier has reduced the prover's AND-constraint satisfaction to these three equalities, to which we turn now.

Unrolling the right-hand sides using the definitions of those quantities [already given](/blueprint/ands/oblong/univariate), and also rearranging a few of those expressions' sums, we get the following three claims:

$$
\begin{align*}
\alpha_a \stackrel{?}= \sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_\text{op} \sum_{y \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell_\text{words}}} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}_6} \delta_D(r'_{\widehat{i}}, \widehat{i}) \cdot \widetilde{A}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, y, s) \cdot \widetilde{\textsf{shift-ind}}_\text{op}(i, j, s) \cdot \widetilde{w}(j, y), \\
\alpha_b \stackrel{?}= \sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_\text{op} \sum_{y \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell_\text{words}}} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}_6} \delta_D(r'_{\widehat{i}}, \widehat{i}) \cdot \widetilde{B}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, y, s) \cdot \widetilde{\textsf{shift-ind}}_\text{op}(i, j, s) \cdot \widetilde{w}(j, y), \\
\alpha_c \stackrel{?}= \sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_\text{op} \sum_{y \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell_\text{words}}} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}_6} \delta_D(r'_{\widehat{i}}, \widehat{i}) \cdot \widetilde{C}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, y, s) \cdot \widetilde{\textsf{shift-ind}}_\text{op}(i, j, s) \cdot \widetilde{w}(j, y).
\end{align*}
$$

## A Factorization

At this point, we _could_ just tell the prover to "run the sumcheck algorithm". The problem is that this sum is enormous: it is over $\ell_\text{words} + 18$ variables. Its raw table of values would thus be something like $2^{18}$ times as large as the witness, which is absurd and unacceptable.

The essential point for us is that that not all variables appear in all functions. If they did, we'd have no choice but to run the standard algorithm. As it happens, though, each function has only some of the variables $I$, $J$, $S$, and $Y$. Moreover, the way that these variables are "distributed" allows some very interesting things to happen.

Let us explain. First, note that for each $s$, $j$ and $\text{op}$, among the four inner polynomials, two of them contain $i$ but not $y$ (namely $\delta_D(r'_{\widehat{i}}, \widehat{i})$ and $\widetilde{\textsf{shift-ind}}_\text{op}(i, j, s)$), and the other two contain $y$ but not $i$ (namely $\widetilde{A}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, y, s)$ and $\widetilde{w}(j, y)$, and the same for $B$ and $C$).

Because of this, we can factor the innermost nested sums over $i$ and $y$ into a product of two sums, for each $s$, $j$ and $\text{op}$. In this way, we get the following factorizations:

$$
\begin{align*}
\alpha_a \stackrel{?}= \sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_\text{op} \Bigg[\sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}_6} \delta_D(r'_{\widehat{i}}, \widehat{i}) \cdot \widetilde{\textsf{shift-ind}}_\text{op}(i, j, s)  \Bigg] \cdot \Bigg[ \sum_{y \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell_\text{words}}} \widetilde{A}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, y, s) \cdot \widetilde{w}(j, y) \Bigg], \\
\alpha_b \stackrel{?}= \sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_\text{op} \Bigg[\sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}_6} \delta_D(r'_{\widehat{i}}, \widehat{i}) \cdot \widetilde{\textsf{shift-ind}}_\text{op}(i, j, s)  \Bigg] \cdot \Bigg[ \sum_{y \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell_\text{words}}} \widetilde{B}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, y, s) \cdot \widetilde{w}(j, y) \Bigg], \\
\alpha_c \stackrel{?}= \sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_\text{op} \Bigg[\sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}_6} \delta_D(r'_{\widehat{i}}, \widehat{i}) \cdot \widetilde{\textsf{shift-ind}}_\text{op}(i, j, s)  \Bigg] \cdot \Bigg[ \sum_{y \in \mathcal{C}_{\ell_\text{words}}} \widetilde{C}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, y, s) \cdot \widetilde{w}(j, y) \Bigg].
\end{align*}
$$

This wouldn't do us much good, if it weren't for the following further observation. In each of the three expressions above, both bracketed quantities are _multilinear_ in both $J$ and $S$.

Indeed, each left-hand bracketed quantity is multilinear in $j$ and $s$, since those variables appear only in $\widetilde{\textsf{shift-ind}}_\text{op}(i, j, s)$ and not in $\delta_D(r'_{\widehat{i}}, \widehat{i})$. Similarly, each right-hand bracketed expression is also multilinear in $j$ and $s$, since $\widetilde{A}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, y, s)$ depends on $s$ but not $j$ while $\widetilde{w}(j, y)$ depends on $j$ but not $s$.

As a result, we can treat each expression above as a sum, over the cube $\mathcal{B}_6 \times \mathcal{B}_6$, of a multivariate polynomial in $J$ and $S$ expressible as the sum of three products of multilinears (i.e., over varying $\text{op}$).

We cover more details in the next segment.

## The First Phase

The above remarks suggest the following first step. As a purely notational matter, we introduce some abbreviations. For each $\text{op} \in \{\mathsf{sll}, \mathsf{srl}, \mathsf{sra}\}$, we define the following 12-variate multilinears on $J$ and $S$:

$$
\begin{equation*}h_\text{op}(J, S) \coloneqq \sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}_6} \delta_D(r'_{\widehat{i}}, \widehat{i}) \cdot \widetilde{\textsf{shift-ind}}_\text{op}(i, J, S);\end{equation*}
$$

similarly, for each $\text{op}$, we define:

$$
\begin{align*}
g_{a, \text{op}}(J, S) &\coloneqq \sum_{y \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell_\text{words}}} \widetilde{A}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, y, S) \cdot \widetilde{w}(J, y), \\
g_{b, \text{op}}(J, S) &\coloneqq \sum_{y \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell_\text{words}}} \widetilde{B}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, y, S) \cdot \widetilde{w}(J, y), \\
g_{c, \text{op}}(J, S) &\coloneqq \sum_{y \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell_\text{words}}} \widetilde{C}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, y, S) \cdot \widetilde{w}(J, y),
\end{align*}
$$

Crucially, everything above is multilinear in $J$ and $S$. moreover, by definition, our claims amount to:

$$
\begin{align*}
\alpha_a \stackrel{?}= \sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_\text{op} h_\text{op}(j, s) \cdot g_{a, \text{op}}(j, s), \\
\alpha_b \stackrel{?}= \sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_\text{op} h_\text{op}(j, s) \cdot  g_{b, \text{op}}(j, s), \\
\alpha_c \stackrel{?}= \sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_\text{op} h_\text{op}(j, s) \cdot g_{c, \text{op}}(j, s).
\end{align*}
$$

At this point, the verifier can freely sample three random scalars, say $\lambda_a$, $\lambda_b$, and $\lambda_c$, and send them to the prover. Up to tiny soundness error, it thus suffices for the parties to define $\alpha_0 \coloneqq \lambda_a \cdot \alpha_a + \lambda_b \cdot \alpha_b + \lambda_c \cdot \alpha_c$, and assess the sum claim:

$$
\begin{equation*}\alpha_0 \stackrel{?}= \sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{B}_6} \sum_\text{op} h_\text{op}(j, s) \cdot \Big[ \lambda_a \cdot g_{a, \text{op}}(j, s) + \lambda_b \cdot g_{b, \text{op}}(j, s) + \lambda_c \cdot g_{c, \text{op}}(j, s) \Big].\end{equation*}
$$

Thus, the prover can begin by tabulating the $2^6 \times 2^6$ tables of values of all three functions $h_\text{op}(J, S)$, and all three functions $\lambda_a \cdot g_{a, \text{op}}(J, S) + \lambda_b + g_{b, \text{op}}(J, S) + \lambda_c \cdot g_{c, \text{op}}(J, S)$, so 6 functions total (i.e., we are letting $\text{op}$ vary). The total resulting data here is of size $2^6 \cdot 2^6 \cdot 6$ field elements, or 384 KiB total. In the [next page](/blueprint/ands/reduction/considerations), we talk about how the prover can prepare these tables of values efficiently, exploiting the sparsity of the constraint system.

In any case, skipping that for now, we can tell the parties to run the sumcheck above. The sumcheck is 12-dimensional, over a sum of three products of multilinears (i.e., taken over $\text{op}$). After 12 rounds, the parties will have reduced the prover's claim to the further claim:

$$
\begin{equation*}\alpha_{12} \stackrel{?}= \sum_\text{op} h_\text{op}(r_j, r_s) \cdot \Big[ \lambda_a \cdot g_{a, \text{op}}(r_j, r_s) + \lambda_b \cdot g_{b, \text{op}}(r_j, r_s) + \lambda_c \cdot g_{c, \text{op}}(r_j, r_s) \Big],\end{equation*}
$$

for 6-dimensional random scalars $r_j$ and $r_s$ sampled during the sumcheck.

## The Second Phase

At this point, we rename $\gamma_0 \coloneqq \alpha_{12}$. Rearranging the claim we left off with above, by unwinding what the evaluations $g_{a, \text{op}}(r_j, r_s)$, $g_{b, \text{op}}(r_j, r_s)$ and $g_{c, \text{op}}(r_j, r_s)$ mean, we get:

$$
\begin{align*}\gamma_{0} &\stackrel{?}= \sum_\text{op} h_\text{op}(r_j, r_s) \cdot \Big[ \sum_{m \in \{a, b, c\}}\lambda_m \cdot g_{m, \text{op}}(r_j, r_s) \Big] \\
&= \sum_\text{op} h_\text{op}(r_j, r_s) \cdot \Big[ \sum_{y \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell_\text{words}}} \Big( \sum_{m \in \{a, b, c\}} \lambda_m \cdot \widetilde{M}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, y, r_s) \Big) \cdot \widetilde{w}(r_j, y) \Big] \\
&= \sum_{y \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell_\text{words}}} \left[ \sum_\text{op} h_\text{op}(r_j, r_s) \cdot \sum_{m \in \{a, b, c\}} \lambda_m \cdot \widetilde{M}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, y, r_s) \right] \cdot \widetilde{w}(r_j, y).
\end{align*}
$$

Above, we use a typographical device where we write $m$ and $M$ for "one of" the symbols $a$, $b$ and $c$, $A$, $B$ and $C$ and so on.

In any case, we can now view the _above_ expression as a further sumcheck, now over the single $\ell_\text{words}$-dimensional variable $y$. The entire bracketed expression can be viewed as a single multilinear in $Y$; the partial specialization $\widetilde{w}(r_j, Y)$ is of course also multilinear in $Y$. Again [in the next page](/blueprint/ands/reduction/considerations), we go into how the prover can tabulate the necessary values efficiently.

For now, we instruct the parties to run an $\ell_\text{words}$-round sumcheck on the above. After doing that, they wind up with a further claim of the form:

$$
\begin{equation*}\gamma_{\ell_\text{words}} \stackrel{?}= \left[ \sum_\text{op} h_\text{op}(r_j, r_s) \cdot \sum_{m \in \{a, b, c\}} \lambda_m \cdot \widetilde{M}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, r_y, r_s) \right] \cdot \widetilde{w}(r_j, r_y),\end{equation*}
$$

where $r_y$ is a further value sampled during the sumcheck.

We work under the assumption that the verifier can evaluate the entire bracketed quantity himself. We have already [talked at great length](/blueprint/ands/indicators) about how the verifier can efficiently, locally, compute the evaluations $h_\text{op}(r_j, r_s)$ himself. Our case is a bit different than what we previously covered, since the verifier will need to evaluate $\textsf{shift-ind}_\text{op}(i, r_j, r_s)$ for _each_ $i \in \mathcal{B}_6$, as opposed to just at a single point. But the idea is similar. The verifier can carry out this computation in a way whose cost grows linearly in the size of $\mathcal{B}_6$ (64 for us, obviously), and with small constants. The concrete cost is very low.

It remains for the verifier to evaluate $\widetilde{A}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, r_y, r_s)$, $\widetilde{B}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, r_y, r_s)$, and $\widetilde{C}_{\text{and}, \text{op}}(r'_x, r_y, r_s)$ for each $\text{op}$. This is a bit trickier. There is a straightforward way for the verifier to do this that takes $O(n_\text{words}) + O(n_\text{and}) + O(\text{circuit size})$ work, which we again cover in the [next page](/blueprint/ands/reduction/considerations). This cost is practically tolerable for us. If this had to be improved—and in particular made fully succinct—the Spark compiler [[Set20](https://eprint.iacr.org/2019/550)] is suitable for this kind of thing.

In any case, it remains for the verifier to output the single evaluation claim on $\widetilde{w}(r_j, r_y)$. This is exactly the kind of thing that our [PCS](/blueprint/commitment) is designed to treat, so this completes our treatment of the AND reduction, at least from the verifier's point of view.
