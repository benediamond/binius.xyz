# Towers of Binary Fields [A crash course on our basic datatypes]

Binius uses key mathematical objects called _towers of binary fields_. These are integral to everything we do. In this page, we explain them, _starting from the user's perspective_. Indeed, towers appear _both_ in our [frontend](/basics/arithmetization) _and_ in our [backend](/blueprint/reductions). Leaving aside mathematics and cryptography, then, even _users_ of Binius need to understand just a tiny bit about them, at least as formal objects.

## The Datatypes

Our [arithmetization scheme](/basics/arithmetization), called _M3_ (short for _multi-multiset matching_), uses tower fields as its fundamental datatypes. Before getting into that scheme, we sketch a few basics about these datatypes.

In short, our fundamental datatypes are bitstrings of power-of-two width. Thus, we allow 128-bit binary strings, 64-bit strings, 32-bit strings, and so on, down to 8-bit bytes. We also allow 1-bit values. (For technical reasons, we skip the 4-bit and 2-bit widths.)

We also need to be able to talk about _adding_ and _multiplying_ strings like these. For us, addition is just bitwise XOR. Multiplication is more complicated; we refrain from explaining it for now.

Throughout, we will use symbols like $\mathcal{T}_\iota$ to refer to these datatypes. For now, $\mathcal{T}_\iota$ means "the datatype consisting of binary strings of width $2^\iota$".

:::info
For example, $\mathcal{T}_7$ is the datatype consisting of 128-bit binary strings. We will often refer to this as the "cryptographically large field" or the "huge field". $\mathcal{T}_0$ is the datatype consisting of 1-bit values (bits). $\mathcal{T}_3$ is the datatype of bytes.
:::

:::note
We note that our binary datatypes are arguably _simpler_ than those associated with prime fields. For example, [RISC Zero](https://risczero.com/)'s arithmetization uses the _Baby Bear_ prime field $\mathbb{F}_p$, where $p = 2^{31} - 2^{27} + 1$. Concretely, element of $\mathbb{F}_p$ look like integers in the range $\{0, \ldots , p - 1\}$; arithmetic is done modulo $p$.

These integers take _almost_ 32 bits to represent—but not quite. Indeed, at the level of bits, these integers look essentially like 32-bit binary strings—with the caveat that strings representing integers in the upper range $\{p, \ldots , 2^{32} - 1\}$ are not allowed. Elements in this range are called "non-canonical", since by subtracting off some multiples of $p$, we can replace them with elements of the canonical range $\{0, \ldots , p - 1\}$.

In Binius, the field $\mathcal{T}_\iota$'s elements map _exactly_ to the set of length-$2^\iota$ bitstrings, in a perfect one-to-one correspondence. There are no "forbidden" or "non-canonical" bitstrings.
:::

## Binary Towers

Under the hood, our power-of-two-width binary strings actually represent elements of algebraic objects called _binary towers_. We emphasize that the algebraic story underneath these objects is _not_ necessary for consumers of Binius—those interested in writing circuits and programs. On the other hand, it will ultimately prove necessary as we progress into our backend, so we record some details here.

Our towers $\mathcal{T}_\iota$ are mathematical sets called _fields_. Indeed, each tower $\mathcal{T}_\iota$ is isomorphic _as a field_ to $\mathbb{F}_{2^{2^\iota}}$, the field of order $2^{2^\iota}$. Importantly, though, we construct these fields in a special way, which gives them a "nested" structure and makes everything work nicely.

### Field extensions

The basic concept here is a _field extension_. Let's start with the simplest possible field, $\mathbb{F}_2$—the field with just the elements $0$ and $1$. Given some _extension degree_ $k$, it is a classical technique to construct the _field extension_ $\mathbb{F}_{2^k} \mathbin{/} \mathbb{F}_2$. Usually, the way we do this is by fixing a degree-$k$ _irreducible polynomial_ $f_k(X) \in \mathbb{F}_2[X]$. Our field is then given as the quotient ring $\mathbb{F}_{2^k} \cong \mathbb{F}_2[X] \mathbin{/} \left( f_k(X) \right)$; that is, as the set of univariate polynomials over $\mathbb{F}_2$ _modulo_ the ideal generated by the irreducible polynomial $f_k(X)$. Concretely, elements of this ring can be represented as polynomials over $\mathbb{F}_2$ of degree _less than_ $k$. Addition is just componentwise addition in $\mathbb{F}_2$ (i.e., XOR). To multiply two elements, we multiply them _as binary polynomials_, and then reduce the result modulo $f_k(X)$.

In practice, we will represent elements of the ring $\mathbb{F}_2[X] \mathbin{/} \left( f_k(X) \right)$ as $k$-bit binary strings, by writing down their _coefficients_. In slightly fancier terms, this amounts to picking an $\mathbb{F}_2$-basis for this ring (here, we're simultaneously viewing it also as $\mathbb{F}_2$-vector space). Which basis? Well, the obvious one: the set of monomials $1, X, X^2, \ldots , X^{k - 1}$.

### Towers of extensions

In Binius, we take a different approach. Starting as before with $\mathcal{T}_0 \coloneqq \mathbb{F}_2$, we take just a _degree-$2$_ extension:

$$
\begin{equation*}\mathcal{T}_1 \coloneqq \mathcal{T}_0[X_0] \mathbin{/} \left( X_0^2 + X_0 + 1 \right).\end{equation*}
$$

As a field, $\mathcal{T}_1$ is clearly just $\mathbb{F}_{2^2} = \mathbb{F}_4$.

On the other hand, blackboxing the definition of $\mathcal{T}_1$—i.e., forgetting for now that it's _in its own right_ a quotient ring—we can repeat this process:

$$
\begin{equation*}\mathcal{T}_2 \coloneqq \mathcal{T}_1[X_1] \mathbin{/} \left( X_1^2 + X_0 \cdot X_1 + 1 \right).\end{equation*}
$$

We state without proof for now that $X_1^2 + X_0 \cdot X_1 + 1$ is irreducible over $\mathcal{T}_1$. Thus, $\mathcal{T}_2$ is also a field—namely, isomorphic to $\mathbb{F}_{2^4}$.

:::info
What do elements of $\mathcal{T}_2$ look like? Well, they look like _linear_ polynomials, say $\alpha_0 + \alpha_1 \cdot X_1$, in $X_1$, where each _coefficient_ is itself a $\mathcal{T}_1$-element (that is, a a linear polynomial in its own right in the indeterminate $X_0$, with coefficients in $\mathcal{T}_0 = \mathbb{F}_2$).

For example, let's say that $\alpha_0 = 1 + X_0$ and $\alpha_1 = X_0$. Thus, we have:

$$
\begin{equation*}\alpha = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 \cdot X_1 = \left( 1 + X_0 \right) + \left( X_0 \right) \cdot X_1.\end{equation*}
$$

By expanding and rearranging, we can express everything as a _two-variate_ polynomial in $X_0$ and $X_1$:

$$
\begin{equation*}\alpha = 1 + X_0 + X_0 \cdot X_1.\end{equation*}
$$

Of course, this thing is multilinear (each indeterminate only appears with exponent at most 1). Moreover, every polynomial that arises in this way will be too.
:::

We can continue this process by writing, for _each_ $\iota \geq 2$,

$$
\begin{equation*}\mathcal{T}_\iota \coloneqq \mathcal{T}_{\iota - 1}[X_{\iota - 1}] \mathbin{/} \left( X_{\iota - 1}^2 + X_{\iota - 2} \cdot X_{\iota - 1} + 1 \right).\end{equation*}
$$

In this way, we get a chain, or _tower_, of extensions $\mathcal{T}_0 \subset \cdots \subset \mathcal{T}_\iota$, and so on.

Elements of $\mathcal{T}_\iota$ look like _linear polynomials_ over $\mathcal{T}_{\iota - 1}$—whose coefficients are themselves polynomials over $\mathcal{T}_{\iota - 2}$, and so on. Unwinding this process, we get a kind of recursive, tree-like composition of elements. At the bottom of the tree are the bits. Indeed, this also shows that $\mathcal{T}_\iota$ has an $\mathbb{F}_2$-basis given by the _multilinear monomials_ in the indeterminates $X_0, \ldots , X_{\iota - 1}$ (there are $2^\iota$ such monomials). We will call this the _multilinear monomial basis_.

### Relative bases

There is a further very important idea hidden here. To explain it, we fix two _different_ tower subfields $\mathcal{T}_\iota \subset \mathcal{T}_\tau$. Elements of $\mathcal{T}_\tau$ look like linear polynomials over $\mathcal{T}_{\tau - 1}$, whose _coefficients_ are linear polynomials over $\mathcal{T}_{\tau - 2}$, and so on. We can perform the same unwinding we just did above, but _stop_ at the intermediate height $\mathcal{T}_\iota$. In this way, we can express each given $\mathcal{T}_\tau$-element as a multilinear polynomials in the indeterminates $X_\iota, \ldots , X_{\tau - 1}$, _whose coefficients_ come from $\mathcal{T}_\iota$. We thus see that the multilinear monomials in $X_\iota, \ldots , X_{\tau - 1}$ give us a basis for $\mathcal{T}_\tau$ over its nontrivial subfield $\mathcal{T}_\iota$. (This is a generalization of what we just did above, in which the ground field can be something intermediate, as opposed to $\mathbb{F}_2$ itself.) We will also refer to this as the _multilinear monomial basis_ of $\mathcal{T}_\tau$ over $\mathcal{T}_\iota$.
