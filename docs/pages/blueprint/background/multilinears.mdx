# Multilinear Polynomials [The existence and uniqueness of multilinear extensions]

For each [field](/blueprint/background/towers) $\mathcal{T}_\iota$, we write $\mathcal{T}_\iota[X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}]$ for the ring of multivariate polynomials in the indeterminates $X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}$. A polynomial $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) \in \mathcal{T}_\iota[X_0, \ldots, X_{\ell - 1}]$ is _multilinear_ if each indeterminate appears in it with _individual_ degree at most 1.

Here, we explain an important mathematical duality that we will use throughout this site. That is:

:::note
_Multilinear polynomials_ and _lists of values_ are two sides of the same coin.
:::

## Multilinears and Lists

More precisely, for each field $\mathcal{T}_\iota$ and each size parameter $\ell$, we have two objects which are really "the same":

1. A multilinear polynomial $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ with coefficients in $\mathcal{T}_\iota$.
2. An ordered list of $2^\ell$ elements of $\mathcal{T}_\iota$.

To go from 1. to 2. above, we actually take two steps. We begin with a multilinear $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ over $\mathcal{T}_\iota$. First, we can pick up its "Lagrange coordinates"—that is, its restriction to the cube $\mathcal{B}_\ell \subset \mathcal{T}_\tau^\ell$. Indeed, we have the cube-indexed vector of values $\left( t(v) \right)_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell}$.

We can then flatten the cube $\mathcal{B}_\ell$ itself, by associating it with the _ordered_ list of integers $\{0, \ldots , 2^\ell - 1\}$. Indeed, we can associate each $v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell \mapsto \{ v \} \coloneqq \sum_{i = 0}^{\ell - 1} 2^i \cdot v_i$. Thus, if we define $t_{\{v\}} \coloneqq t_v$ for each $v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell$, then we get the desired list $(t_i)_{i = 0}^{2^\ell - 1}$. It's not hard to see that this list is just $\left( t(0, \ldots , 0), t(1, 0, \ldots , 0), \ldots , t(0, 1, \ldots , 1), t(1, \ldots , 1) \right)$ (i.e., the evaluation points are taken in lexicographic order).

In the next couple steps, we discuss going in the _reverse direction_ (i.e., from 2. to 1.).

## The Equality Indicator Polynomial

We begin by recording a key polynomial. For each fixed $\ell \geq 0$, the $2 \cdot \ell$-variate _equality indicator polynomial_ $\widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}, Y_0, \ldots , Y_{\ell - 1})$ is defined to be:

$$
\begin{equation*}\widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}, Y_0, \ldots , Y_{\ell - 1}) \coloneqq \prod_{i = 0}^{\ell - 1} \left( (1 - X_i) \cdot (1 - Y_i) + X_i \cdot Y_i \right).\end{equation*}
$$

This polynomial, on the one hand, is multilinear in its $2 \cdot \ell$ variables. On the other hand, its _restriction_ to the cube $\mathcal{B}_\ell \times \mathcal{B}_\ell$ recovers the _equality indicator function_, the function which, on two bitstrings $u$ and $v$, equals 1 if and only if $u = v$ and 0 otherwise.

This polynomial has two key properties:

- **It is succinct.** Indeed, given any pair of points $r$ and $r'$ in $\mathcal{T}_\tau^\ell$, the evaluation $\widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r, r')$ can be computed in $O(\ell)$ work. (Indeed, we can work one at a time through the $\ell$ factors of the right-hand side above, and then multiply them all together.) This is _not_ true for generic, $2 \cdot \ell$-variate multilinear polynomials! Indeed, $\widetilde{\texttt{eq}}$ is special. (To get a hint for _what's_ special about it, just look at how it _factors_ into a product of $\ell$ terms—not all polynomials will do this.)
- **Its partial specializations give us Lagrange basis polynomials.** Indeed, if we pick a cube point $v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell$, nothing stops us from partially specializing $\widetilde{\texttt{eq}}$'s later variables $(Y_0, \ldots , Y_{\ell - 1})$ to $(v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - 1})$. Doing this gives us the $\ell$-variate polynomial $(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) \mapsto \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}, v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - 1})$. Now this polynomial has two important properties. First, it's multilinear in the variables $X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}$. Second, its restriction to the cube $\mathcal{B}_\ell$ is _identically zero except for one point_, namely the point $v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell$. This makes it useful as a "building block", as we'll see just below.

## Multilinear Extensions

As we'll argue now, the association between the list items 1. and 2. above is _bijective_. That is, we already saw that each multilinear polynomial determines a list of values on the cube (i.e., we went from 1. to 2.). What we want to do is reverse this process: beginning with a list of values $\left( t_i \right)_{i = 0}^{2^\ell - 1}$, we want to reverse-engineer a multilinear $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ whose evaluations $\left( t(v) \right)_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell}$ on the cube recover the initial list $\left( t_i \right)_{i = 0}^{2^\ell - 1}$.

It turns out that the desired multilinear $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$:

- **Exists.** Indeed, to recover $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$, we reverse the process above. Beginning with the ordered list $\left( t_i \right)_{i = 0}^{2^\ell - 1}$, we can arrange the list elements onto the cube vertices by taking $\left( t_{\{v\}} \right)_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell}$. Next, we need to "extend" this mapping into a polynomial. It turns out we can use the _Lagrange basis polynomials_ just introduced. That is, we can take the combination
  $$
  \begin{equation*}t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) \coloneqq \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} t_{\{v\}} \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - 1}, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}).\end{equation*}
  $$
  You can check that this candidate $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ indeed fulfills our desideratum: namely, its values on the cube satisfy $t(v) = t_{\{v\}}$ for each $v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell$.
- **Is unique.** The slightly trickier part is to argue that the multilinear polynomial $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ is _unique_—that is, it's the _only possible_ multilinear whose restriction to the cube equals $\left( t_i \right)_{i = 0}^{2^\ell - 1}$. To show this, it's enough to argue that if a multilinear vanishes identically on the cube, then it must actually be the 0 polynomial. This proof is elementary but a bit delicate (and pretty interesting); it is carried out for example in Thaler [[Tha23](https://people.cs.georgetown.edu/jthaler/ProofsArgsAndZK.pdf), Fact 3.5.].

Thus, not only can we associate to each multilinear $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ a list of values $\left( t_i \right)_{i = 0}^{2^\ell - 1}$, but this association is reversible: to each list $\left( t_i \right)_{i = 0}^{2^\ell - 1}$, we get _a unique_ multilinear whose evaluations on the cube recover that list.
