# Verifying M3 Instances [How to actually solve M3 problems]

As we've argued, M3 is a flexible _abstraction_ which lets us formulate and capture computational problems. On the other hand, M3 is only as useful as it is amenable to actual cryptographic treatment. How do we solve M3 problems, once we've formulated them? In this page, we discuss how both parties (i.e., prover and verifier) may _reduce_ the satisfaction of M3 instances to problems that our [cryptographic reductions](/reductions) are capable of treating.

We note that we have introduced, above, the roles of _prover_ and _verifier_, which were completely absent from the [abstract defintion](/arithmetization/matching/definition) of M3. In Binius, it will be the _verifier_ who is interested in assessing the adequacy of an M3 attempt by the _prover_. The _verifier_ will pre-seed all channels at hand with statement-specific data, and will orchestrate the execution of the instance's flushing rules. The _prover_ will be responsible for populating the instance's tables in the first place; i.e., he will do so with an eye towards getting the verifier's channels to balance (and cognizant all the while of the attempt's statement, and of the verifier's pre-seeding rules). The goal will be to do all this in such a way that is _succinct_ for the verifier, and moreover reasonably efficient for the prover.

As is justified by our [polynomial commitment scheme](/cryptography), we will work in the abstract _PIOP model_. In this model, the prover can "commit to" multilinear polynomials, by sending them to an abstract oracle machine (which in turn notifies the verifier, upon each receipt). Later, the verifer can _query_, or evaluate, these polynomials at points of his own choosing, simply by asking the oracle. By this model's very definition, we assume that this oracle is "perfectly reliable": once the prover has committed to a polynomial, he can't change it; moreover, the verifier is guaranteed to get the correct evaluations out of the oracle.

## Checking the Attempt

To check whether an M3 _attempt_ satisfies an M3 _instance_, we need to ensure two major things.

### Constraint satisfaction

Here, we need to check whether the the prover has populated the instance's various _tables_ in such a way that their _constraints_ are satisfied. The rough point here is that this constraint-satisfaction check amounts to a [zerocheck](/reductions/zerocheck), and can be checked as such.

We are going to begin with a very familiar and standard concept: the duality between height-$2^\ell$ columns of $\mathcal{T}_\iota$-elements, on the one hand, and $\ell$-variate multilinear polynomials over $\mathcal{T}_\iota$, on the other. Indeed, up to selecting an ordering of the cube's axes, both the prover and verifier may freely lexicographically identify $\{0, \ldots , 2^\ell - 1\}$ with $\mathcal{B}_\ell$, and hence identify table columns with $\ell$-variate multilinears. (Indeed, each $\ell$-variate multilinear is determined exactly by its values on the cube.)

In view of this identification, our first step is clear: our prover must _commit_ to all of the instance's table's columns, interpreting each as a multilinear (over the appropriate number of variables and over the appropriate field).

:::info
Here, we have a subtlety about the _heights_ of the attempt's tables: will they all be powers of 2? The answer in general is _no_; and this leads to an important source of technical complexity in our implementation. For our purposes now, we suppress this issue.
:::

To make this slightly more formal, we introduce notation. We fix a particular table, populated during a prover attempt in such a way that it has $2^\ell$ rows, let's say. We write $M_0, \ldots M_{t - 1}$ for this table's _columns_, suitably reinterpreted as multilinears; here, each $M_i(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ is an $\ell$-variate multilinear over an appropriate field (i.e., the field of definition of the relevant column). Finally, we write $C_0, \ldots , C_{c - 1}$ for the table's _constraints_. Each of these is a $t$-variate polynomial, defined over the indeterminates $Z_0, \ldots , Z_{t - 1}$, say. The table's satisfaction of its constraints amounts to the condition whereby, for each constraint $j \in \{0, \ldots , c - 1\}$, the $\ell$-variate multivariate polynomial

$$C_j(M_0(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}), \ldots, M_{t - 1}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}))$$

vanishes identically on the cube $\mathcal{B}_\ell$.

This is _precisely_ the scenario that the [zerocheck](/reductions/zerocheck) is designed to treat. The _zerocheck_ serves to reduce identical vanishing claims of the above form to _evaluation_ claims. That is, by executing a certain sumcheck, the verifier may reduce the vanishing claim above to further _evaluation claims_—that is, to claims of the form $M_i(r) \stackrel{?}= s_i$ (where the evaluation point $r$ and the claimed values $s_i$ arise during the zerocheck). By definition of the PIOP model, we already agreed that the verifier can evaluate these claims directly. We give much more detail in our [reductions](/reductions) and [cryptography](/cryptography) sections.

### Channel balancing

As we hinted at when we [defined](/arithmetization/matching/definition) M3, channels, under the hood, are, _essentially_ pairs of dueling "multisets". A _multiset_ is a list of elements, in which the unique items are counted, as is the _number_ of times each item appears (its _multiplicity_), but the order of the elements is not counted.
