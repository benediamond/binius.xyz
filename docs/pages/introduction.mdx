 # Binius [A SNARK protocol over towers of binary fields, implemented in Rust]

_Binius_ is a state-of-the-art noninteractive succinct proof. It comprises a theoretical cryptographic protocol (expounded in a sequence of recent technical manuscripts [[1]](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1784) [[2]](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/504)), a production-grade [Rust implementation](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius) of that protocol, and finally a documentation suite, featured on this very website.

Binius's underlying cryptographic protocol operates natively over _towers of binary fields_, mathematical objects which, on the one hand, efficiently arithmetize natural problems of interest (like standard hash functions), and, on the other, admit extremely efficient implementations in both software and hardware. Binius's full codebase resides on GitLab at [`IrreducibleOSS / binius`](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/). That codebase contains not just Binius's core prover and verifier logic, but moreover various examples which serve to exhibit that system's end-to-end behavior on tasks of essential interest (like Merkle–Patricia trie inclusion proving).

## Site Directory

Below, we sketch this site's main sub-areas in slightly more detail. Binius, in sum, spans roughly three separate layers of abstraction, which we outline now.

<Callout type="note" className="hover:bg-highlight">
<Collapsible>
[**The arithmetization layer.**](/arithmetization) In this layer—the outermost—we define abstract tools and objects which serve to efficiently capture _meaningful_ real-life statements and problems.

Binius's arithmetization scheme departs radically from those of other SNARKs. Like other schemes, ours features PLONK-like _tables_, to each of which arbitrary polynomial _gate constraints_—operating row-wise—may be imposed identically. Departing from other schemes, we link and connect our various tables using a new abstraction we call _channels_. Channels are abstract objects, _to_ and _from_ which tuples of data may be "pushed" and "pulled". Our arithmetization paradigm encompasses _not just_ a plurality of tables (each, again, bearing gate constraints), but moreover so-called _flushing rules_, which dictate how elements from these tables should be pushed to and pulled from channels. A channel is said to be _balanced_ when the pushes made to it exactly match the pulls made from it.

Our arithmetization scheme supports problems whose satisfaction corresponds exactly to the _balancing_ of a plurality of channels, themselves populated according to flushing rules from pre-arranged tables. Indeed, the _balancedness_ of any particular channel may be expressed as a multiset-equality problem; for this reason, we call our channel-based paradigm _multi-multiset matching_. For more on arithmetization, start [here](/arithmetization).
</Collapsible>
</Callout>

<Callout type="note" className="hover:bg-highlight">
<Collapsible>
[**The cryptographic reductions.**](/reductions) This layer contains a plurality of reduction techniques, aimed largely at progressively _simplifying_ and _unifying_ claims output by the arithmetization layer.

Indeed, our arithmetization layer serves to reduce _satisfiability_ problems to cryptographic claims, themselves either _multiset equality_ claims or _vanishing_ claims (these sorts of claims respectively correspond to the balancing of channels and to the satisfaction of gate constraints).

Our _reduction layer_ serves to progressively unroll, simplify, and unify these sorts of cryptographic claims. More specifically, the layer's goal is to reduce these cryptographic claims—which amount to complex predicates on _committed_ and _virtual_ polynomials—to simple _evaluation claims_. An _evaluation claim_, by definition, entails that a particular committed multilinear, at a particular point, evaluates to a particular value. These are the sorts of claims that are amenable to direct evaluation by our PCS. More specifically, our goal is to further reduce our claim at hand to the a plurality of evaluation claims, which moreover pertain to _as few different points_ as possible (this latter condition makes batch evaluation applicable).

Our cryptographic reduction layer is described in detail [here](/reductions).
</Collapsible>
</Callout>

<Callout type="note" className="hover:bg-highlight">
<Collapsible>
[**The polynomial commitment scheme.**](/cryptography) In this layer reside the cryptographic constructions and techniques which make it possible to securely commit to—and later, to evaluate—multilinear polynomials over tiny fields.

This layer contains production-grade implementations of the the multilinear polynomial commitment schemes described in [[1]](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1784) and [[2]](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/504). Though we defer our schemes' correctness and security analyses to those papers, our documentation of this layer serves to explain what our code does, and how it relates to those theoretical works.
</Collapsible>
</Callout>
