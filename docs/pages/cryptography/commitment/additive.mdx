# The Additive NTT [Binary Reed–Solomon codes, and an efficient algorithm to encode them]

The most important code we come across in SNARKs is the _[Reed–Solomon code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reed%E2%80%93Solomon_error_correction)_. This is a simple—and yet extremely powerful and effective—code, and appears not just in Binius but also in [DEEP-ALI](https://eprint.iacr.org/2019/336.pdf)-based SNARKs like [Plonky3](https://github.com/Plonky3/Plonky3) and [Stwo](https://github.com/starkware-libs/stwo).

Reed–Solomon codes are not _just_ coding-theoretically nice. They also support an extremely rich set of auxiliary protocols, designed to achieve such ends as _proximity testing_ (determining whether a supplied _word_ is close to a codeword or not, by just reading from it at a few places). The crucial protocol here is _[FRI](https://eccc.weizmann.ac.il/report/2017/134/)_ (short for _fast Reed–Solomon interactive oracle proof of proximity_), due to Ben-Sasson, Bentov, Horesh and Riabzev.

## Review of Reed–Solomon Codes

Mathematically, each Reed–Solomon code depends on a _field_ $\mathbb{F}$, a _message length_ $k$ and a _block length_ $n$. We first need to pick a _domain_ $S \subset \mathbb{F}$ of size $|S| = n$ (as a side effect, we need $| \mathbb{F} | \geq n$ for this to make sense).

Our _message space_ will be the set of univariate polynomials $P(X) = a_0 + a_1 \cdot X + \cdots + a_{k - 1} \cdot X^{k - 1}$ of degree less than $k$ with coefficients in $\mathbb{F}$. The _word space_ will be the set of all $n$-tuples of $\mathbb{F}$-elements $(b_0, \ldots , b_{n - 1})$ (here, we can think of associating these with the elements of $S$). The _codewords_ will be those $n$-tuples which arise as the set of evaluations of some polynomial $P(X)$ as above: that is, those of the form $(P(x_0), \ldots , P(x_{n - 1}))$, where $x_i$ ranges throughout $S$.

## The Problem

In Binius, Reed–Solomon codes also play a central role. On the other hand, since we work with binary fields, things get significantly trickier. The first question that arises has to do with _encoding_: the actual algorithm which takes as input a message and outputs its corresponding codeword. As should be clear from the above, this is a problem of _polynomial evaluation_: how do we take the $k$ coefficients of some polynomial, and output the $n$ values of that polynomial on some domain? We can trivially do this in $O(k \cdot n)$ steps; the goal is to do better than that.

In the classical, prime-field setting, encoding is very standard: the key algorithm is the [NTT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_Fourier_transform_over_a_ring#Number-theoretic_transform) (_number-theoretic transform_), a close relative of the [fast Fourier transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fast_Fourier_transform). The NTT runs in time quasilinear in the length of the message (and linear in the ratio of the codeword's length to the message's length).

In binary fields, primitive multiplicative roots of unity of power-of-two order don't exist, and things get significantly trickier. Indeed, it was not known until 2014—i.e., until the arrival of a key work by Lin, Chung and Han [[LCH14](https://arxiv.org/abs/1404.3458)]—how to encode in the _binary_ setting in such a way as to recover, or match, the time complexity of the classical case.

:::note
On the other hand, [[LCH14](https://arxiv.org/abs/1404.3458)] culminated a rich line of predecessor works, including those of Cantor [[Can87](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/0097316589900204)] and Gao–Mateer [[GM10](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/5625613)]. It has also spawned important successor works, like the _Frobenius Additive FFT_ of Li et al. [[Li+18](https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.03932)].
