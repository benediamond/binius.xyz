# High-Level Overview [A summary of Binius's steps, from the verifier's perspective]

In this page, we dump a self-contained, high-level, end-to-end overview of our verifier's steps and stages. These stages serve to reduce an initial statement—say, pertaining to the validity of an Ethereum block—into progressively simpler mathematical claims, culminating in claims about the evaluations of certain multilinear polynomials.

We conduct this overview with a view towards the _verifier's_ perspective, and not the prover's. Indeed, the verifier's view attests to a protocol's soundness, while the prover's view attests to its completness (and both views reflect its efficiency!). Here, we aim merely to sketch why Binius delivers a sound proof. Throughout, we roughly follow the structure of Binius's [main verification file](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/verify.rs?ref_type=heads).

## Interaction, _Fiat–Shamir_, and Reductions

Binius operates within the _Fiat–Shamir paradigm_ for noninteractive proofs. Our protocol, abstractly, proceeds through multiple, broad "phases", _each_ in turn encompassing multiple rounds of interaction between the prover and the verifier. As is standard in the practice of noninteractive proofs, we replace all interaction in our protocol by invocations of the random oracle (concretely, a hash function).

We achieve this using an abstract object called a _transcript_. On the one hand, the transcript tracks all data sent by the prover to the verifier; simultaneously, it moreover hashes this data, automating the process by which the verifier's challenges are derived. When the verifier verifies a proof, it uses a parallel transcript object, which absorbs the prover's sent data, and moreover independently produces the correct challenge sequence.

Our protocol's respective phases can be viewed as cryptographic _reductions_. Each phase serves to _reduce_ the truth of claims of one sort to the truth of claims of a simpler sort. (For example, our GKR-based _grand product_ protocol can reduce a claim about the _product_ of a multilinear's values over the cube to a claim about its _evaluation_ at a random point.) By proceeding in this way through our protocol's various phases—which we summarize below—our verifier may, steadily, reduce the prover's initial statement into simpler and simpler claims, until everything is finally proven.

## The M3 Instance

Before beginning any given Binius proof, the prover and verifier must agree in advance on the structure of the statement being proved. The `ConstraintSystem` object in Binius, which the prover and verifier share, serves to fully specify the statement at hand. Each `ConstraintSystem`, conceptually, represents an instance of the _M3_ (_multi-multiset matching_) problem. Our codebase moreover contains a `ConstraintSystemBuilder` utility, which allows the parties to intuitively build M3 instances.

We explain M3 at great length in our [arithmetization section](/arithmetization). For now, we simply sketch the constraint system's components. Indeed, the constraint system entails a set of _oracles_, abstract handles that represent multilinear polynomials, as well as multivariate polynomial _constraints_, which these oracles must satisfy in any acceptable proof. Finally, the system establishes _flushing rules_, which dictate how exactly oracles should be flushed to any among a number of abstract objects called _channels_. As we explain in our [arithmetization page](/arithmetization), the M3 instance is _satisfied_ if and only if the prover manages to populate the instance's tables in such a way as to cause all channels to balance. As we further argue in that section, M3 is an extremely rich and flexible paradigm, and serves to _efficiently_ capture statements of critical real-life interest.

Taking for granted the M3 abstraction, we proceed into verifier's algorithms proper. In what follows, we sketch the steps by which the verifier may evaluate whether the prover's M3 instance is satisfying, by reducing its satisfaction to successively simpler claims.

## Receiving Commitments

Before it generates any randomness, the verifier must receive the prover's commitments and absorb them into its transcript. Each "commitment" represents a massive, multilinear polynomial—or equivalently, a column in a table—which, for efficiency's sake, is hidden from the verifier (part of the M3 instance's _witness_). For the sake of succinctness, instead of conveying these columns directly to the verifier, the prover rather conveys _commitments_ to them; these commitments "bottle up" the polynomials they represent. Concretely, each commitment looks like a single hash digest. As soon as the prover forks over these commitments to the verifier, it becomes effectively bound to the witness polynomials they conceal. We discuss our multilinear polynomial commitment scheme further in this site's [cryptography page](/cryptography).

## The Zerocheck

The prover's _commitments_, discussed above, represent the _values_ with which it has opted to populate the M3 instance's various tables. To each among these tables is associated various _constraints_, multivariate polynomials which must vanish identically, row-wise, on the table's rows (i.e., by definition of the M3 instance). The verifier's first task conceptually is to make sure that the relevant constraints _actually hold_ row-wise on the prover's tables.

The _zerocheck_ is the key tool for this problem. The _zerocheck protocol_ reduces _vanishing claims_ for composites of multilinears to _evaluation claims_ on the underlying multilinears. The classical zerocheck technique is essentially a thin wrapper around the sumcheck. Basically, if we want to check that the $\ell$-variate multivariate composite polynomial $C(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ is such that $C(v) = 0$ holds for each $v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell$, then it's enough to define $C$'s _multiilnear extension_:

$$\widetilde{C}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) \coloneqq \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} C(v) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}, v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - 1})$$,

and check whether $\widetilde{C}(r) \stackrel{?}= 0$ holds, for a point $r \gets L^\ell$ sampled randomly by the verifier. The key trick is that the claim $\widetilde{C}(r) \stackrel{?}= 0$ itself, in turn, amounts to a _sumcheck claim_, and can be checked using that protocol. Here,

$$\widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}, Y_0, \ldots , Y_{\ell - 1}) \coloneqq \prod_{i = 0}^{\ell - 1} \left( (1 - X_i) \cdot (1 - Y_i) + X_i \cdot Y_i \right)$$

is the _equality indicator function_, the unique, $2 \cdot \ell$-variate multilinear polynomial whose restriction to $\mathcal{B}_\ell \times \mathcal{B}_\ell$ is the equality indicator function. More precisely, the _sumcheck protocol_ reduces the above sum claim—that is, $0 \stackrel{?}= \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} C(v) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r, v)$—to an _evaluation claim_ on the underlying polynomial $C$ (that is, to a claim of the form $C(r') \stackrel{?}= s$, where the values $r'$ and $s$ arise during the sumcheck protocol).

We discuss the zerocheck further [here](/reductions/zerocheck). Because we work over small fields, we in fact adopt a rather sophisticated [variant](/reductions/zerocheck/univariate) of the zerocheck, called the _univariate skip_. Very roughly, this zerocheck treats the 0th round in a special way, and moves as much work as possible into that round.

## The Channel-Balancing Argument

At the center of each M3 instance is a plurality of abstract objects called _channels_, which must _balance_ if the proof is to pass. The verifier's next few steps involve establishing that the prover's channels balance—or more precisely, reducing the balancing of these channels to _further_ evaluation claims. (These will accumulate, for now, alongside those already output by the zerocheck reduction sketched above.)

Under the hood, each channel amounts to a pair of dueling "multisets", abstract lists populated by the instance's flushing rules. To check whether the relevant channels balance, the verifier must therefore run a handful of _multiset checks_. The theory behind these checks is outlined fully in Protocols 4.17 and 4.20 of [[DP23](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1784.pdf)], and is discussed further [here](/reductions/multiset). For now, we sketch the idea. We write $(T_0, \ldots , T_{\mu - 1})$ and $(U_0, \ldots , U_{\mu - 1})$ for a single multiset instance; that is, we want to check that

$$\left\{ (T_0(v), \ldots , T_{\mu - 1}(v)) \mid v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell \right\} = \left\{ (U_0(v), \ldots , U_{\mu - 1}(v)) \mid v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell \right\}$$,

where both sides are viewed as _multi-subsets_ of $\mathcal{T}_\tau^\mu$.

What [[DP23](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1784.pdf), § 4] tells us is that to check this claim, _it suffices in turn_ (up to some negligible soundness error) for the verifier to sample a _fingerprinting challenge_ $s$ and a _permutation challenge_ $r$, and to check instead that the virtual polynomials:

$$T^* \coloneqq r + \sum_{i = 0}^{\mu - 1} s^i \cdot T_i$$ and $$U^* \coloneqq r + \sum_{i = 0}^{\mu - 1} s^i \cdot U_i$$

have identical _grand products_. That is, it's enough to check that $\prod_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} T^*(v) \stackrel{?}= \prod_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} U^*(v)$.

## The GKR-Based Grand Product Argument

In light of the above reduction, the balancing of the M3 instance's channels has now been reduced to an equality of _grand products_. The verifier's next step is to _further_ reduce the grand product claims at issue to evaluation claims. To achieve this, the verifier undertakes a "grand product argument", itself based on the _GKR protocol_.

We describe our GKR-based grand product argument in detail [here](/reductions/gpa); for now, we roughly sketch the idea. We write $T^*$ for the multilinear whose grand product we want to ascertain—let's say, we want to check whether $\prod_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} T^*(v) \stackrel{?}= a$ or not. To do this, the prover and verifier both imagine a height-$\ell$, balanced binary tree, whose leaves are the values $\left\{T^*(v) \mid v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell \right\}$, and whose intermediate nodes are defined to be the products of their children. Clearly, it suffices for the verifier to check whether the tree's root note is $a$. The idea of GKR is that for _each_ tree layer $k \in \{0, \ldots , \ell\}$, we can view the $2^k$ values of that layer's nodes as the values of some multilinear $T^*_k$ over the cube $\mathcal{B}_k$. Moreover, for each $k \in \{0, \ldots , \ell - 1\}$ and each evaluation point $r_k \in L_k$, we obtain the following expression for $T^*_k$, in terms of $T^*_{k + 1}$:

$$T^*_k(r_k) = \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_k} T^*_{k + 1}(v \mathbin{\Vert} 0) \cdot T^*_{k + 1}(v \mathbin{\Vert} 1) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(v, r_k)$$.

By sumchecking this thing, the verifier can reduce the claim $T^*_k(r_k) \stackrel{?}= a_k$ to the further claim $T^*_{k + 1}(r_{k + 1}) \stackrel{?}= a_{k + 1}$, for some further values $r_{k + 1}$ and $a_{k + 1}$ obtained during the sumcheck. By performing this repeatedly, we get a way to reduce the initial grand product claim $a \stackrel{?}= T_0(\varnothing) = \prod_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} T^*(v)$ to an evaluation claim of the form $a_\ell \stackrel{?}= T_\ell(r_\ell) = T(r_\ell)$.

We've thus reduced all of our grand product claims to evaluation claims, which is what we wanted.
