# High-Level Overview [A summary of Binius's steps, from the verifier's perspective]

In this page, we dump a self-contained, high-level, end-to-end overview of our verifier's steps and stages. These stages serve to reduce an initial statement—say, pertaining to the validity of an Ethereum block—into progressively simpler mathematical claims, culminating in claims about the evaluations of certain multilinear polynomials.

We conduct this overview with a view towards the _verifier's_ perspective, and not the prover's. Indeed, the verifier's view attests to a protocol's soundness, while the prover's view attests to its completness (and both views reflect its efficiency!). Here, we aim merely to sketch why Binius delivers a sound proof. Throughout, we roughly follow the structure of Binius's [main verification file](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/verify.rs?ref_type=heads).

## The M3 Instance

In each Binius proof, the prover and verifier must agree in advance on a [`ConstraintSystem`](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/mod.rs?ref_type=heads#L32-39). From the verifier's viewpoint, a "constraint system" amounts to the specification of an _M3_ (_multi-multiset matching_) instance. We explain M3 at great length in our [arithmetization section](/arithmetization). For now, we simply itemize the constraint system's components. Indeed, the constraint system entails a set of [_oracles_](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/oracle/multilinear.rs?ref_type=heads#L226-269), abstract handles that represent multilinear polynomials, as well as multivariate polynomial [_constraints_](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/oracle/constraint.rs?ref_type=heads#L17-20), which these oracles must satisfy in any acceptable proof. Finally, the system establishes [_flushing rules_](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/channel.rs?ref_type=heads#L60-64), which dictate how exactly oracles should be flushed to any among a number of abstract [_channels_](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/channel.rs?ref_type=heads#L57). As we explain in our [arithmetization page](/arithmetization), the M3 instance is _satisfied_ if and only if the prover manages to populate the instance's tables in such a way as to cause all channels to balance. As we further argue in that section, M3 is an extremely rich and flexible paradigm, and serves to _efficiently_ capture statements of critical real-life interest.

Our verifier makes explicit reference to the relevant constraint system during its proof, for example [here](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/verify.rs?ref_type=heads#L149-150) and [here](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/verify.rs?ref_type=heads#L167-173).

Taking for granted the M3 abstraction, we proceed into verifier's algorithms proper. In what follows, we sketch the steps by which the verifier may evaluate whether the prover's M3 instance is satisfying.

## Receiving Commitments

Before it generates any randomness, the verifier must receive and "observe" the prover's commitments. Each "commitment" represents a massive, multilinear polynomial—or equivalently, a column in a table—which is hidden from the verifier (part of the M3 instance's _witness_). For the sake of succinctness, instead of conveying these columns directly to the verifier, the prover rather conveys _commitments_ to them; these commitments "bottle up" the polynomials they represent. Concretely, each commitment looks like a single hash digest. As soon as the prover forks over these commitments to the verifier, it becomes effectively bound to the witness polynomials underneath them. We discuss our multilinear polynomial commitment scheme further in this site's [cryptography page](/cryptography).

As is mandated by the Fiat–Shamir heuristic, the verifier, upon receiving the prover's commitments, must hash them into its "transcript" (we call this process _observation_). Concretely, the prover's commitments are destructured [here](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/verify.rs?ref_type=heads#L102); they are absorbed into the verifier's transcript [here](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/verify.rs?ref_type=heads#L141).

## The Channel-Balancing Argument

At the core of each M3 instance is a plurality of abstract objects called _channels_, which must _balance_ if the proof is to pass. The verifier's next few steps involve establishing that the prover's channels balance. Under the hood, each channel amounts to a pair of dueling "multisets", abstract lists populated by flushing rules.

To check whether the relevant channels balance, the verifier must run a handful of _multiset checks_. To do this, it samples a [fingerprinting challenge](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/verify.rs?ref_type=heads#L144) and a handful of [permutation challenges](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/verify.rs?ref_type=heads#L146) (see Protocols 4.17 and 4.20 of [[DP23]](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1784.pdf)). Using these, the verifier materializes [certain virtual oracles](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/verify.rs?ref_type=heads#L149-150)—heeding the constraint system's flushing rules—whose grand products control whether the system's channels balance.

Using the grand-product claims [supplied nondeterministically](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/verify.rs?ref_type=heads#L103-104) by the prover, the verifier [kicks off](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/verify.rs?ref_type=heads#L153-157) an execution of the GKR-based [grand product argument](/). By verifying the prover's GKR proofs, the verifier "unwinds" these initial grand product claims, reducing their validity, in turn, to certain [evaluation claims](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/verify.rs?ref_type=heads#L158-162) on underlying multilinears.

Separately, the verifier [checks](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/verify.rs?ref_type=heads#L164) that the product claims nondeterministically supplied by the prover actually represent balancing channels.

## The Zerocheck

For the M3 instance to be satisfied, not only must the prover's tables balance the system's channels, but its tables must moreover satisfy the system's _constraints_. Constraints are essentially multivariate polynomials, each with as many variables as the number of columns of the table upon which it acts. A table is said to _satisfy_ a constraint if that constraint vanishes identically on each of the table's rows.

The _zerocheck_ is the key tool for this problem. Zerocheck reduces _vanishing claims_ for composites of multilinears to _evaluation claims_ on the underlying multilinears. The classical zerocheck technique is essentially a thin wrapper around the sumcheck.

Because we work over small fields, we in fact adopt a rather sophisticated [variant](/) of the zerocheck, called the _univariate skip_. Very roughly, this zerocheck treats the 0th round in a special way, and moves as much work as possible into that round. Our verifier's zerocheck-specific work takes place in [this block](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/verify.rs?ref_type=heads#L178-227) of verifier code.
