# High-Level Overview [A summary of Binius's steps, from the verifier's perspective]

In this page, we dump a self-contained, high-level, end-to-end overview of our verifier's steps and stages. These stages serve to reduce an initial statement—say, pertaining to the validity of an Ethereum block—into progressively simpler mathematical claims, culminating in claims about the evaluations of certain multilinear polynomials.

We conduct this overview with a view towards the _verifier's_ perspective, and not the prover's. Indeed, the verifier's view attests to a protocol's soundness, while the prover's view attests to its completness (and both views reflect its efficiency!). Here, we aim merely to sketch why Binius delivers a sound proof. Throughout, we roughly follow the structure of Binius's [main verification file](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/constraint_system/verify.rs?ref_type=heads).

## Interaction, _Fiat–Shamir_, and Reductions

Binius operates within the _Fiat–Shamir paradigm_ for noninteractive proofs. Our protocol, abstractly, proceeds through multiple, broad "phases", _each_ in turn encompassing multiple rounds of interaction between the prover and the verifier. As is standard in the practice of noninteractive proofs, we replace all interaction in our protocol by invocations of the random oracle (concretely, a hash function).

We achieve this using an abstract object called a _transcript_. On the one hand, the transcript tracks all data sent by the prover to the verifier; simultaneously, it moreover hashes this data, automating the calculation of the verifier's challenges. When the verifier verifies a proof, it uses a parallel transcript object, which absorbs the prover's sent data, and moreover independently produces the correct challenge sequence.

Our protocol's respective phases can be viewed as cryptographic _reductions_. Each phase serves to _reduce_ the truth of claims of one sort to the truth of claims of a simpler sort. By proceeding in this way through our protocol's various phases—which we summarize below—our verifier may, steadily, reduce the prover's initial statement into simpler and simpler claims, until everything is finally proven.

## The M3 Instance

Before beginning any given Binius proof, the prover and verifier must agree in advance on the structure of the statement being proved. Binius operates in the _M3_ arithmetization paradigm, which we explain at great length in our [arithmetization section](/arithmetization). For now, we simply sketch the objects upon which the prover and verifier must agree.

Each M3 instance is captured by a concrete object called a _constraint system_. Each constraint system entails a set of _oracles_, abstract handles that represent multilinear polynomials, as well as multivariate polynomial _constraints_, which these oracles must satisfy in any acceptable proof. Finally, the system establishes _flushing rules_, which dictate how exactly oracles should be flushed to any among a number of abstract objects called _channels_. As we explain when we [define M3](/arithmetization/matching/definition), the M3 instance is _satisfied_ if and only if the prover manages to populate the instance's tables in such a way as to cause all channels to balance. As we further argue in that section, M3 is an extremely rich and flexible paradigm, and serves to _efficiently_ capture statements of critical real-life interest.

Taking for granted the M3 abstraction, we proceed into verifier's algorithms proper. In what follows, we sketch the steps by which the verifier may evaluate whether the prover's M3 instance is satisfying, by reducing its satisfaction to successively simpler claims.

## Receiving Commitments

Before it generates any randomness, the verifier must receive the prover's commitments and absorb them into its transcript. Each "commitment" represents a massive, multilinear polynomial—or equivalently, a column in a table—which, for efficiency's sake, is hidden from the verifier (part of the M3 instance's _witness_). For the sake of succinctness, instead of conveying these columns directly to the verifier, the prover rather conveys _commitments_ to them; these commitments "bottle up" the polynomials they represent. Concretely, each commitment looks like a single hash digest. As soon as the prover forks over these commitments to the verifier, it becomes effectively bound to the witness polynomials they conceal. We discuss our multilinear polynomial commitment scheme further in this site's [cryptography page](/cryptography).

## Zerocheck

The prover's _commitments_, discussed above, represent the _values_ with which it has opted to populate the M3 instance's various tables. To each among these tables is associated various _constraints_, multivariate polynomials which must hold identically, row-wise, on the table's rows (i.e., by definition of the M3 instance). The verifier's first task conceptually is to make sure that the relevant constraints _actually hold_ row-wise on the prover's tables.

The _zerocheck_ is the key tool for this problem. The _zerocheck protocol_ reduces _vanishing claims_ for composites of multilinears to _evaluation claims_ on the underlying multilinears. The classical zerocheck technique is essentially a thin wrapper around the sumcheck. Basically, if we want to check that the $\ell$-variate multivariate composite polynomial $C(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ is such that $C(v) = 0$ holds for each $v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell$, then it's enough to define $C$'s _multiilnear extension_:

$$\widetilde{C}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) \coloneqq \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} C(v) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}, v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - 1})$$,

and check whether $\widetilde{C}(r) \stackrel{?}= 0$ holds, for a point $r \gets \mathcal{T}_\tau^\ell$ sampled randomly by the verifier. The key trick is that the claim $\widetilde{C}(r) \stackrel{?}= 0$ itself, in turn, amounts to a _sumcheck claim_, and can be checked using that protocol. Here,

$$\widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}, Y_0, \ldots , Y_{\ell - 1}) \coloneqq \prod_{i = 0}^{\ell - 1} \left( (1 - X_i) \cdot (1 - Y_i) + X_i \cdot Y_i \right)$$

is the _equality indicator polynomial_, the unique, $2 \cdot \ell$-variate multilinear polynomial whose restriction to $\mathcal{B}_\ell \times \mathcal{B}_\ell$ is the equality indicator function. More precisely, the _sumcheck protocol_ reduces the above sum claim—that is, $0 \stackrel{?}= \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} C(v) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r, v)$—to an _evaluation claim_ on the underlying polynomial $C$ (that is, to a claim of the form $C(r') \stackrel{?}= s$, where the values $r'$ and $s$ arise during the sumcheck protocol).

We discuss the zerocheck further [here](/reductions/zerocheck). Because we work over small fields, we in fact adopt a rather sophisticated variant of the zerocheck, called the [_univariate skip_](/reductions/zerocheck/univariate). Very roughly, this zerocheck treats the 0th round in a special way, and moves as much work as possible into that round.

## The Channel-Balancing Argument

At the center of each M3 instance is a plurality of abstract objects called _channels_, which must _balance_ if the proof is to pass. The verifier's next few steps involve establishing that the prover's channels balance. More precisely, the verifier must reduce the balancing of these channels to _further_ evaluation claims. (These latter claims will accumulate, for now, alongside those already output by the zerocheck reduction sketched above.)

Under the hood, each channel amounts to a pair of dueling "multisets", abstract lists populated by the instance's flushing rules. To check whether the relevant channels balance, the verifier must therefore run a handful of _multiset equality checks_. The theory behind these checks is developed in Protocols 4.17 and 4.20 of [[DP23](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1784.pdf)], and is discussed further [here](/reductions/multiset). For now, we sketch the idea. We write $(T_0, \ldots , T_{\mu - 1})$ and $(U_0, \ldots , U_{\mu - 1})$ for a single multiset instance; that is, we want to check that

$$\left\{ (T_0(v), \ldots , T_{\mu - 1}(v)) \mid v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell \right\} = \left\{ (U_0(v), \ldots , U_{\mu - 1}(v)) \mid v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell \right\}$$,

where both sides are viewed as _multi-subsets_ of $\mathcal{T}_\tau^\mu$.

What [[DP23](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1784.pdf), § 4] tells us is that to check this claim, it suffices (up to some negligible soundness error) for the verifier to sample a _fingerprinting challenge_ $s$ and a _permutation challenge_ $r$, and to check instead that the virtual polynomials:

$$T^* \coloneqq r + \sum_{i = 0}^{\mu - 1} s^i \cdot T_i$$ and $$U^* \coloneqq r + \sum_{i = 0}^{\mu - 1} s^i \cdot U_i$$

have identical _grand products_. That is, it's enough to check that $\prod_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} T^*(v) \stackrel{?}= \prod_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} U^*(v)$.

## The GKR-Based Grand Product Argument

In light of the above reduction, the balancing of the M3 instance's channels has now been reduced to an equality of _grand products_, or products of the values of certain polynomials over the cube. The verifier's next step is to _further_ reduce the grand product claims at issue to evaluation claims. To achieve this, the verifier undertakes a "grand product argument", itself based on the _GKR protocol_.

We describe our GKR-based grand product argument in detail [here](/reductions/gpa); for now, we roughly sketch the idea. We write $T$ for a multilinear whose grand product we want to ascertain—that is, we want to check whether $\prod_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} T(v) \stackrel{?}= a$, let's say. To do this, the prover and verifier both imagine a height-$\ell$, balanced binary tree, whose leaves are the values $\left\{T(v) \mid v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell \right\}$, and whose intermediate nodes are defined to be the products of their children. Clearly, it suffices for the verifier to check whether the tree's root note is $a$. The idea of GKR is that for _each_ tree layer $k \in \{0, \ldots , \ell\}$, we can view the $2^k$ values of that layer's nodes as the values of some multilinear $T_k$ over the cube $\mathcal{B}_k$. Moreover, for each $k \in \{0, \ldots , \ell - 1\}$ and each evaluation point $r_k \in \mathcal{T}_\tau^k$, we obtain the following expression for $T_k$, in terms of $T_{k + 1}$:

$$T_k(r_k) = \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_k} T_{k + 1}(v \mathbin{\Vert} 0) \cdot T_{k + 1}(v \mathbin{\Vert} 1) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(v, r_k)$$.

By sumchecking this thing, the verifier can reduce the claim $T_k(r_k) \stackrel{?}= a_k$ to the further claim $T_{k + 1}(r_{k + 1}) \stackrel{?}= a_{k + 1}$, for some further values $r_{k + 1}$ and $a_{k + 1}$ obtained during the sumcheck. By performing this repeatedly, we get a way to "unwind" the initial grand product claim $a \stackrel{?}= T_0(\varnothing) = \prod_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} T(v)$ into an evaluation claim of the form $a_\ell \stackrel{?}= T_\ell(r_\ell) = T(r_\ell)$.

We've thus reduced all of our grand product claims to evaluation claims, which is what we wanted.

## Evalcheck

Throughout the zerocheck protocol and the channel-balancing phases above, the verifier's goal was to progressively reduce the prover's M3 satisfaction to _evaluation claims_ on multilinears. Here, we have finally reached that point. At this point in the protocol, we pass into a new phase, called _evalcheck_, in which we begin processing these evaluation claims.

The main problem is that the multilinears we want to evaluate are, in general, committed _or_ virtual. Roughly, the verifier's task during evalcheck will be to progressively "unroll" the definitions of the _virtual_ multilinears at hand, thereby reducing its running evaluation claims on them to claims on further, committed, multilinears.

_Virtual_ multilinears play an essential role in Binius. For further detail, we refer to our [page](/reductions/virtual) devoted exclusively to them, as well as to [[DP23](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1784.pdf), § 4.1]. Here, we sketch the idea. Given access to various _underlying_ concrete, committed multilinears, the verifier may use these as "building blocks" to construct further, _virtual_ polynomials in its head. For example, given evaluation access to $A(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$, the verifier can "simulate" evaluation access to its scaling $t'(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) \coloneqq g \cdot t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ (here, $g \in \mathcal{T}_\tau$ is some constant scalar). How? Easily: if the verifier wants to learn the value of $t'$ at $r \in \mathcal{T}_\tau^\ell$, say, it may query the underlying polynomial $t(r)$, and then locally multiply the result by $g$.

As trivial as this idea sounds, it rapidly gets serious in Binius. For example, as we explain in [[DP23](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1784.pdf), § 4.3], it is possible, given some fixed multilinear $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$, to construct a further virtual multilinear—called $\texttt{shift}_{\ell, o}(t)(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$—whose values on the cube $\mathcal{B}_\ell$ are just those of $t$, but _circularly rotated_ (by $o$ steps in this case). Moreover, the verifier may reduce the problem of evaluating $\texttt{shift}_{\ell, o}(t)$ at some arbitrary point $r \in \mathcal{T}_\tau^\ell$ to that of evaluating the underlying polynomial $t$ at some further point $r' \in \mathcal{T}_\tau^\ell$, up to the cost of doing an $\ell$-round sumcheck.

For a full explanation of how this gets done, we refer to our [dedicated page](/reductions/virtual/shift) on the matter. For now, we sketch it. Indeed, we get an expression:

$$\texttt{shift}_{\ell, o}(t)(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) = \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} t(v) \cdot \texttt{s-ind}_{\ell, o}(v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - 1}, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$$.

Here, $\texttt{s-ind}_{\ell, o}$ is a special, $2 \cdot \ell$-variate multilnear whose restriction to $\mathcal{B}_{\ell} \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell}$ is the _circular shift indicator function_ (i.e., it's 1 on $(u, v)$ if and only if $u$ and $v$ are $o$ arithmetic steps apart, and 0 otherwise). The above expression shows that, for each evaluation point $r \in \mathcal{T}_\tau^\ell$, the evaluation $\texttt{shift}_{\ell, o}(t)(r)$ can itself be expressed as the sum over the cube of the product polynomial $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) \cdot \texttt{s-ind}_{\ell, o}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}, r_0, \ldots , r_{\ell - 1})$. By the aid of a sumcheck, we can reduce this sum to, in turn, evaluation claims on the underlying polynomials $t$ and $\texttt{s-ind}_{\ell, o}$. The essential, and non-trivial, point is that $\texttt{s-ind}_{\ell, o}$ itself has an efficient circuit representation, and can be evaluated _locally_ at any point by the verifier in just $O(\ell)$ time. So the verifier just needs to query $t$ once.

This idea gives us lots of mileage, since, by setting the offset $o \coloneqq 1$ for example, we can shift a column by one step—and match the output of one row to the input of the next, let's say.

In any case, during evalcheck, the verifier runs various sumchecks, progressively reducing evaluation claims on _virtual_ things like $\texttt{shift}_{\ell, o}(t)$ to evaluation claims on _committed_ underlying polynomials like $t$ itself. We describe evalcheck in full [here](/reductions/evalcheck).

## The Opening Phase

We're now finally ready to do some cryptography! At this point, the verifier has reduced the problem of checking the satisfaction of the prover's M3 instance to that of checking the evaluations of various committed multilinears at various points. Here, the verifier is going to directly evaluate these latter evaluation claims, by invoking our core polynomial commitment scheme (PCS). The full resource for this phase of the protocol appears [here](/cryptography).

Binius uses the multilinear polynomial commitment scheme described in [[DP24](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/504)]. The thing that makes it fairly tricky is that we have to do an involved sort of _batching_, which operates over polynomials not just over different coefficient fields, but moreover of different lengths (i.e., numbers of variables). The key idea that we use relates to _ring-switching_, an idea expounded at length in [[DP24](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/504), § 4].

We summarize the idea in the following way. We write $t$ for an $\ell$-variate multilinear over the small field $\mathcal{T}_\iota$, say, and suppose that we're interested in the evaluation claim $t(r) \stackrel{?}= s$. We moreover write $t'$ for $t$'s "packing". The packed polynomial $t'$ is a reinterpretation of $t$ as a polynomial over the huge field $\mathcal{T}_\tau$. Its coefficients are larger, but it has fewer of them—it has only $\ell'$ variables, let's say, as opposed to $\ell$.

As is explained in [[DP24](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/504), § 4], it turns out that, up to a bit of interaction—in which the prover sends the verifier a "tensor algebra" element, and the verifier samples some random challenges—the prover and verifier may mutually reduce their initial evaluation claim $t(r) \stackrel{?}= s$ to a _sum claim_, involving $t'$ and a further $\ell'$-variate multilinear, $A$. Here, $A$ is "transparent", in that it's succinctly evaluable locally by the verifier.

To unwind what that means, our sum claim looks like:

$$s_0 \stackrel{?}= \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell'}} t'(v) \cdot A(v)$$.

We've already seen a few times that the _sumcheck_ can be used to reduce this thing to an evaluation of $t'$. Instead of proceeding that way, though, we're first going to _batch_ the respective sum claims—i.e., each of the above form—that come from _all_ of our initial evaluation claims. We're finally going to integrate this into FRI-Binius's FRI-folding procedure, so that we only have to do that once. The full details are available in our [cryptography section](/cryptography).
