# Merging [Building large polynomials out of smaller ones]

As we've discussed a few times already, multilinear polynomials can be associated with lists of values. That is, for each field $\mathcal{T}_i$ and each size parameter $\ell$, we have two objects which are really "the same":

1. A multilinear polynomial $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ with coefficients in $\ell$.
2. An ordered list of $2^\ell$ elements of $\mathcal{T}_\iota$.

Indeed, we can go from 1. to 2. in two steps. First, we may associate $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ with its vector of values on the cube $\left( t(v) \right)_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell}$. We can then flatten the cube $\mathcal{B}_\ell$ itself, by associating it with the _ordered_ list of integers $\{0, \ldots , 2^\ell - 1\}$ (indeed, we can just associate $v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell \mapsto \{ v \} \coloneqq \sum_{i = 0}^{\ell - 1} 2^i \cdot v_i$). Thus we finally get the list $\left( t(0, \ldots , 0), t(1, 0, \ldots , 0), \ldots , t(1, \ldots , 1) \right)$. To go in the opposite direction, we can associate the list of values $\left( t_i \right)_{i = 0}^{2^\ell - 1}$ with the multilinear extension $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) \coloneqq \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} t_{\{v\}} \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - 1}, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$.

It's a basic mathematical fact that this association is bijective (i.e., $t$'s values on the cube uniquely capture it as a multilinear).

Thus, if we want to talk about _building_ virtual polynomials out of concrete ones, then we can always freely choose to work in the language of lists. That is, we can work in the side 2. of the dictionary above. Of course, everything we do on this side will have an equivalent counterpart on the other side (and vice versa).

## The Basic Example

As an extremely basic example, let's say we have 2 $\ell$-variate things $t_0(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ and $t_1(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$. Equivalently, we have two lists $\left( t_{0, 0}, \ldots , t_{0, 2^\ell - 1} \right)$ and $\left( t_{1, 0}, \ldots , t_{1, 2^\ell - 1} \right)$. What if we want the $\ell + 1$-variate polynomial—say, $t^*(X_0, \ldots , X_\ell)$—associated with the concatenation $\left( t_{0, 0}, \ldots , t_{0, 2^\ell - 1}, t_{1, 0}, \ldots , t_{1, 2^\ell - 1} \right)$? The basic fact is that we have the algebraic representation:

$$t^*(X_0, \ldots , X_\ell) = (1 - X_\ell) \cdot t_0(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) + X_\ell \cdot t_1(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$$.

It is a basic exercise that the list representation of this polynomial $t^*(X_0, \ldots , X_\ell)$ indeed gives us the concatenation of the two lists.

The final thing to note is that the verifier may evaluate $t^*(r)$ efficiently, for any point $(r_0, \ldots , r_\ell) \in \mathcal{T}_\tau^{\ell + 1}$. Indeed, to do this, the verifier should first query $s_0 \coloneqq t_0(r_0, \ldots , r_{\ell - 1})$ and $s_1 \coloneqq t_1(r_0, \ldots , r_{\ell - 1})$. Finally, the verifier should locally compute the combination $(1 - r_\ell) \cdot s_0 + r_\ell \cdot s_1$ and output it. Indeed, this evidently gives us an evaluation procedure for $t^*$, which involves two sub-queries and a bit of local math.

Finally, there is a geometrical story corresponding to this concatenation operation. As the equation above makes clear, $t^*$, as a polynomial, is defined "piecewise" on the $\ell + 1$-dimensional cube. That is, we let the "bottom" $\ell$-subcube of that cube take exactly the values of $t_0$ and the  "top" $\ell$-subcube of that cube take exactly the values of $t_1$.

## Concatenating

It is not hard to generalize this basic procedure to some power-of-two number $2^\alpha$ of constituent polynomials, instead of just 2. We'll fix $\ell$ again, and consider now $\ell$-variate constituent polynomials $t_0, \ldots , t_{2^\alpha - 1}$. To concatenate _all_ of these things into a single, $2^{\ell + \alpha}$-sized list, we want the composite polynomial:

$$t^*(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell + \alpha - 1}) \coloneqq \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\alpha} t_{\{v\}}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(X_\ell, \ldots , X_{\ell + \alpha - 1}, v_0, \ldots , v_{\alpha - 1})$$.

For straightforward reasons, the table of values of $t^*$ over the $\ell + \alpha$-dimensional cube yields exactly the concatenation of the polynomials $t_0, \ldots , t_{2^\alpha - 1}$'s respective individual tables of values. Geometrically, here were are partitioning $\mathcal{B}_{\ell + \alpha}$ into $2^\alpha$ $\ell$-dimensional subcubes, and defining $t^*$ piecewise on $\mathcal{B}_{\ell + \alpha}$ out of the $t_0, \ldots , t_{2^\alpha - 1}$.

The important thing here is that if $2^\alpha$ is small, then this thing is succinct for the verifier. Indeed, to evaluate $t^*$ at some arbitrary point $(r_0, \ldots , r_{\ell + \alpha - 1}) \in \mathcal{T}_\tau^{\ell + \alpha}$, the verifier may first query $s_v \coloneqq t_{\{v\}}(r_0, \ldots , r_{\ell - 1})$, for each $v \in \mathcal{B}_\alpha$. Next, the verifer must simply output the combination $\sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\alpha} s_v \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(v_0, \ldots , v_{\alpha - 1}, r_\ell, \ldots , r_{\ell + \alpha - 1})$. By a standard algorithm, the verifier may compute the vector $\left( \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(v_0, \ldots , v_{\alpha - 1}, r_\ell, \ldots , r_{\ell + \alpha - 1}) \right)_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\alpha}$—and hence the entire sum above—in $\Theta(2^\alpha)$ time.

## Interleaving

By changing this story very slightly, we can also achieve a dual outcome, whereby we _interleave_ $2^\alpha$ $\ell$-variate polynomials. That is, we construct an $\ell + \alpha$-variate polynomial whose (lexicographically flattened) list of evaluations is the interleaving of the respective lists of $2^\alpha$ constituent polynomials $t_0, \ldots , t_{2^\alpha - 1}$.

To do this, we just need to switch the endianness of our combination operation—that is, we need to combine these polynomials using the _low_ variables as "selectors" instead of the high variables. That is, we need to use the alternate expression:

$$t^*(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell + \alpha - 1}) \coloneqq \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\alpha} t_{\{v\}}(X_\alpha, \ldots , X_{\ell + \alpha - 1}) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\alpha - 1}, v_0, \ldots , v_{\alpha - 1})$$.

This thing is also succinct for the verifier to evaluate (provided $2^\alpha$ is small), for the same reason that the concatenated polynomial was.
