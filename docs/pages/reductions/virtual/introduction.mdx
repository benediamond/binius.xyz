# Introduction [Why multilinears are simultaneously lists, and vice versa]

Here, we sketch a mathematical duality that proves essential throughout our treatment of virtual polynomials. Namely: _multilinear polynomials_ and _lists of values_ are two sides of the same coin.

More precisely, for each field $\mathcal{T}_i$ and each size parameter $\ell$, we have two objects which are really "the same":

1. A multilinear polynomial $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ with coefficients in $\mathcal{T}_\iota$.
2. An ordered list of $2^\ell$ elements of $\mathcal{T}_\iota$.

To go from 1. to 2., we actually take two steps. We begin with a multilinear $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ over $\mathcal{T}_\iota$. First, we can pick up its "Lagrange coordinates"—that is, its restriction to the cube $\mathcal{B}_\ell \subset \mathcal{T}_\tau^\ell$. Indeed, we have the cube-indexed vector of values $\left( t(v) \right)_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell}$.

We can then flatten the cube $\mathcal{B}_\ell$ itself, by associating it with the _ordered_ list of integers $\{0, \ldots , 2^\ell - 1\}$. Indeed, we can associate each $v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell \mapsto \{ v \} \coloneqq \sum_{i = 0}^{\ell - 1} 2^i \cdot v_i$. Thus, if we define $t_{\{v\}} \coloneqq t_v$ for each $v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell$, then we get the desired list $(t_i)_{i = 0}^{2^\ell - 1}$. It's not hard to see that this list is just $\left( t(0, \ldots , 0), t(1, 0, \ldots , 0), \ldots , t(0, 1, \ldots , 1), t(1, \ldots , 1) \right)$ (i.e., the evaluation points are taken in lexicographic order).

## Extistence and Uniqueness

It's a basic mathematical fact that this association is _bijective_. That is, we just saw that each multilinear polynomial determines a list of values on the cube (i.e., we went from 1. to 2.). What we want to do is reverse this process: beginning with a list of values $\left( t_i \right)_{i = 0}^{2^\ell - 1}$, we want to reverse-engineer a multilinear $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ whose evaluations $\left( t(v) \right)_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell}$ on the cube recover the initial list $\left( t_i \right)_{i = 0}^{2^\ell - 1}$.

It turns out that the desired multilinear $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$:

- **Exists.** Indeed, to recover $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$, we reverse the process above. Beginning with the ordered list $\left( t_i \right)_{i = 0}^{2^\ell - 1}$, we can arrange the list elements onto the cube vertices by taking $\left( t_{\{v\}} \right)_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell}$. Next, we need to "extend" this mapping into a polynomial. It turns out we can use the _Lagrange basis polynomials_ $\left( \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}, v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - 1}) \right)_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell}$—that is, we can take the combination $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) \coloneqq \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} t_{\{v\}} \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - 1}, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$. As a basic but important exercise, you can check that this candidate $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ indeed fulfills our desideratum: namely, its values on the cube satisfy $t(v) = t_{\{v\}}$ for each $v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell$.
- **Is unique.** The slightly trickier part is to argue that the multilinear polynomial $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ is _unique_—that is, it's the _only possible_ multilinear whose restriction to the cube equals $\left( t_i \right)_{i = 0}^{2^\ell - 1}$. To show this, it's enough to argue that if a multilinear vanishes identically on the cube, then it must actually be the 0 polynomial. This proof is elementary but a bit delicate (and pretty interesting); it is carried out for example in Thaler [[Tha23](https://people.cs.georgetown.edu/jthaler/ProofsArgsAndZK.pdf), Fact 3.5.].

Thus, not only can we associate to each multilinear $t(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ a list of values $\left( t_i \right)_{i = 0}^{2^\ell - 1}$, but this association is reversible: to each list $\left( t_i \right)_{i = 0}^{2^\ell - 1}$, we get _a unique_ multilinear whose evaluations on the cube recover that list.

Thus, if we want to talk about _building_ virtual polynomials out of concrete ones, then we can always freely choose to work in the language of lists. That is, we can work in the side 2. of the dictionary above. As it turns out, the hard part will often to be to jump back to the other side—and to do so in such a way that makes things _efficient_ for the verifier, as we will soon see.
