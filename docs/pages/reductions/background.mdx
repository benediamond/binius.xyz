# Background [Why Binius needs intermediate cryptographic reductions]

In classical, [DEEP-ALI](https://eprint.iacr.org/2019/336.pdf)-based SNARKs like [Plonky3](https://github.com/Plonky3/Plonky3) and [Stwo](https://github.com/starkware-libs/stwo), _all instances more-or-less have the same shape_. We need to pick a field. We need to fix a trace width and a maximal trace height. Finally, we need to write down our AIR instance, which more-or-less looks like crafting a bunch of constraint polynomials (fixed once and for all). _This is the extent_ of the instance-specific parameters which the parties must write down. On a per-statement basis, the prover's job basically looks like filling in a big trace table, and then cranking the handle.

This means that _even across_ AIR instances, the provers' and verifiers' respective jobs more-or-less continue to look the same (they have the same control flow). As instances differ, the only thing that changes is the trace shape, as well as the quantity of and the definitions of the constraint polynomials. On the other hand, the rough sequence of operations of steps remains fixed.

Binius departs radically from this story. In Binius, as we progress across different M3 instances, all manner of things vary. For one, our table and flushing rules both vary, so as to incorporate instance-specific _virtual columns_ (in general). These virtual columns may depend in arbitrarily complex ways—i.e., as the instance demands—on their committed counterparts, and even on each other. In general, we get a _DAG_ (directed acyclic graph) of virtual polynomials, where each _edge_ links some "outer polynomial" to an "inner polynomial" it depends on (terminating with committed or transparent polynomials).

In order to evaluate such a thing, therefore, we must undergo some sequence of unwinding steps. Moreover, as the instance varies, the _shape_ of this DAG will vary; so too, therefore, will the control flow that the prover and the verifer must go through in order to traverse it. Binius contains extremely complex and subtle [logic](/reductions/evalcheck) designed to allow _both parties_ simultaneously and cooperatively to traverse this DAG. Both parties must do so, of course, in a way that is sensitive to the definitions of the various virtual polynomials involved.

Our tables, in general, may contain _both_ committed and virtual columns. The gate constraints and flushing rules which act on these tables, therefore, act in turn upon complex _mixes_, consisting of both committed and virtual columns. After zerochecking the composition polynomial associated with any particular such gate constraint, we thus wind up with _evaluation claims_ on the composition's underlying polynomials (both committed _and_ virtual). In this section, we discuss the portions of Binius which reduce evaluation claims on _virtual_ polynomials to further evaluation claims on _committed_ polynomials. Binius achieves this by progressively "unrolling" the definitions of the virtual polynomials at hand. Since these definition often themselves involve sums, sumchecks ensue. Binius must take responsibility for scheduling, orchestrating, and batching the requisite sumchecks—in such a manner that the prover and verifier can coordinate.

Our use of channels, or _multi-multiset matching_, necessitates that we deal with a further sort of reduction. Indeed, multi-multiset matching outputs _multiset equality_ claims. The multisets at hand—i.e., which each such claim operates on—are themselves virtual; in general, they're complex concatenations of other polynomials, _which themselves_ are virtual in general. Our cryptographic reduction layer, therefore, must moreover handle multiset-equality claims; it must reduce these claims to _grand-product claims_, and—via GKR—ultimately to evaluation claims.
