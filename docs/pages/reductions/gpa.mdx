# GKR-Based Grand Product [A grand-product protocol with no commitment overhead]

As we learned in the context of the [multiset-check](/reductions/multiset), in order for the verifier to assess multiset-equality claims, it's in turn enough for the verifier to assess _grand product claims_. These are claims of the form

$$\prod_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} T(v) \stackrel{?}= a$$;

here, $T$ is $\ell$-variate and _virtual_ in general, and $a$ is held explicitly by the verifier. Here, we explain how we process these sorts of claims—that is, how we reduce them to _evaluation claims_ on $T$.

## Background

The basic idea is that the sumcheck is a _reduction_—it lets us reduce a claim about the _sum over the cube_ of a multivariate polynomial to a claim about the _evaluation_ of that multivariate polynomial. The _GKR protocol_ is a special way of exploiting the sumcheck in a "layered" fashion. That is, we set up a sort of layered circuit or tree. _Each_ layer of that tree is defined elementwise as a sum over the layer below it. Thus to go from one layer to the one beneath it, we need to run a sumcheck, to "peel off" that layer. We end up running as many sumchecks as there are layers in the entire tree.

This technique is very powerful, since many computations of interest can be expressed as layered circuits or trees. As it turns out, multiplication is one of them—and gives us one of our simplest (and most important) applications of GKR.

## The Setup

Let's again write $T(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ for our $\ell$-variate multilinear of interest (itself perhaps virtual). Again, the public data is $a$; our claim is that $\prod_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} T(v) \stackrel{?}= a$.

The idea is very simple: both the prover and verifier will _imagine_ a height-$\ell$, perfectly balanced binary tree. In the leaves, we will inscribe the values $T_\ell(v) \coloneqq T(v)$ for $v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell$ varying (i.e., the leaf layer will be just $T$ itself). For each internal layer $k \in \{0, \ldots , \ell - 1\}$, we will have exactly $2^k$ node values to fill in; we will call these $T_k(v)$, where here $v \in \mathcal{B}_k$ varies. In fact, for each $v \in \mathcal{B}_k$, we will recursively write in the node value $T_k(v) \coloneqq T_{k + 1}(v \mathbin{\Vert} 0) \cdot T_{k + 1}(v \mathbin{\Vert} 1)$. In other words, each internal node will be the product of its two children.

Following this idea to its root (pardon the pun), we see easily that the _root_ of this tree will be $\prod_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} T(v)$, or $a$ itself if the prover is honest. Technically, we should have $T_0(\varnothing) \stackrel{?}= a$ ($T_0$ is a "$0$-variate multilinear", or in other words a constant).

## Unwinding GKR Layers

Now this hasn't gotten us much so far. The key point, though, is that the verifier can use the sumcheck to reduce a claim about _one_ layer to a claim about _the layer below it_. The master claim that the verifier wants to check is a claim about the very top, 0th layer (namely, that $T_0(\varnothing) \stackrel{?}= a$). If the verifier has the ability to repeatedly unwind claims like this, then it may proceed straight down to the leaf layer, at which point it will arrive upon an evaluation claim about $T$ itself, which is what we wanted.

The fact that saves us is that the relationship between each layer and the one below it is _algebraic_. Thus, for each $k \in \{0, \ldots , \ell - 1\}$, since, for each $v \in \mathcal{B}_k$, $T_k(v) = T_{k + 1}(v \mathbin{\Vert} 0) \cdot T_{k + 1}(v \mathbin{\Vert} 1)$ holds by _fiat_, $T_k$'s multilinear extension satisfies:

$$T_k(X_0, \ldots , X_{k - 1}) = \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_k} T_{k + 1}(v_0, \ldots , v_{k - 1}, 0) \cdot T_{k + 1}(v_0, \ldots , v_{k - 1}, 1) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(v_0, \ldots , v_{k - 1}, X_0, \ldots , X_{k - 1})$$.

Each evaluation claim of the form $T_k(r_k) \stackrel{?}= a_k$, say, thus amounts to a _sum claim_ on the $k$-variate, degree-3 multivariate polynomial:

$$T_{k + 1}(X_0, \ldots , X_{k - 1}, 0) \cdot T_{k + 1}(X_0, \ldots , X_{k - 1}, 1) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(X_0, \ldots , X_{k - 1}, r_{k, 0}, \ldots , r_{k, k - 1})$$.

This is exactly the kind of thing we can sumcheck; upon doing so, the verifier will reduce the initial claim $T_k(r_k) \stackrel{?}= a_k$ to a _further_ claim of the form

$$b_k \stackrel{?}= T_{k + 1}(r'_{k, 0}, \ldots , r'_{k, k - 1}, 0) \cdot T_{k + 1}(r'_{k, 0}, \ldots , r'_{k, k - 1}, 1) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r'_k, r_k)$$

for some point $r'_k = (r'_{k, 0}, \ldots , r'_{k, k - 1}) \in \mathcal{T}_\tau^k$ sampled during the sumcheck. The verifier may evaluate $\widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r'_k, r_k)$ itself. As for the others, up to nondeterministically receiving _claimed_ evaluations $s_0 = T_{k + 1}(r'_{k, 0}, \ldots , r'_{k, k - 1}, 0)$ and $s_1 = T_{k + 1}(r'_{k, 0}, \ldots , r'_{k, k - 1}, 1)$ from the prover, the verifier can sample a further random challenge $r'_{k, k} \gets \mathcal{T}_\tau$, and output the reduced evaluation claim $T_{k + 1}(r'_{k, 0}, \ldots , r'_{k, k}) \stackrel{?}= a_{k + 1} \coloneqq (1 - r'_k) \cdot s_0 + r'_k \cdot s_1$.

Thus we've gotten what we wanted: our new evaluation claim is that $T_{k + 1}(r_{k + 1}) \stackrel{?}= a_{k + 1}$. Here, the parties mutually set $r_{k + 1} \coloneqq (r'_{k, 0}, \ldots , r'_{k, k})$ and $a_{k + 1} \coloneqq (1 - r'_k) \cdot s_0 + r'_k \cdot s_1$, just as the verifier did above.

## The End

By proceeding this way throughout $\ell$ rounds, the verifier may thus reduce its initial claim $a \stackrel{?}= T_0(\varnothing) = \prod_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} T(v)$ to the evaluation claim $a_\ell \stackrel{?}= T_\ell(r_\ell) = T(r_\ell)$. Since $T$ is virtual, we agreed already that the verifier will have a way of evaluating it.

Evaluating a virtual polynomial can be complicated, though: a fact which brings us to our next page, [evalcheck](/reductions/evalcheck).
