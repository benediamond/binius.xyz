we have a clear way to express $T_k(v)$ in terms of $T_{k + 1}$:

$$T_k(v) = T_{k + 1}(v \mathbin{\Vert} 0) \cdot T_{k + 1}(v \mathbin{\Vert} 1)$$.

Because $T_k$ is multilinear by _fiat_, we obtain an expression for it in terms of $T_{k + 1}$ by taking the MLE of the right-hand side above:

This trick gives us a way to reduce a claim about $T_k$ to a claim about $T_{k + 1}$. Indeed, the claim $T_k(r_k) \stackrel{?}= a_k$ amounts to the claim:

$$a_k \stackrel{?}= \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_k} T_{k + 1}(v \mathbin{\Vert} 0) \cdot T_{k + 1}(v \mathbin{\Vert} 1) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(v, r_k)$$,

which is exactly the kind of thing we can easily sumcheck. Indeed, after running $k$-sumcheck rounds, the verifier can reduce the claim $T_k(r_k) \stackrel{?}= a_k$ to the further claim $T_{k + 1}(r_{k + 1}) \stackrel{?}= a_{k + 1}$, for some further values $r_{k + 1}$ and $a_{k + 1}$ obtained during the sumcheck. By performing this repeatedly, we get a way to reduce the initial grand product claim $a = a_0 \stackrel{?}= T_0(\varnothing) = \prod_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} T(v)$ to an evaluation claim of the form $a_\ell \stackrel{?}= T_\ell(r_\ell) = T(r_\ell)$.
