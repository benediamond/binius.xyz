# Zerocheck Review [A summary of the classical multilinear vanishing argument]

We use this space to review the _basic_ zerocheck, just so that we can refer to it later. Here, we generally want to fix a column height parameter $\ell \geq 0$, and an $\ell$-variate polynomial $C(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ (not necessarily multilinear!).

:::note
In practice, each multivariate $C(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ we deal with will arise as a _composition of multilinears_; that is, it will come from inlining a set $t_0, \ldots , t_{n - 1}$ of underlying, $\ell$-variate _multilinear_ polynomials into a _composition polynomial_ $c(Z_0, \ldots , Z_{n - 1})$. This fact turns out to be of critical importance from the prover's point of view; we ignore it _for now_.
:::

## The Mathematical Premise

The thing the verifier wants to ensure is that, for each cube point $v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell$, $C(v) = 0$. In other words, the _restriction_ of $C(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ to the cube is identically zero (hence the name _zerocheck_).

The funamental mathematical idea of the zerocheck is that $C$ vanishes on the cube if and only if its multilinear extension $\widetilde{C}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ also does. In fact, more is true:

- **Completeness.** If $C$ vanishes identically on the cube, then $\widetilde{C}$ vanishes _everywhere_ on $\mathcal{T}_\tau^\ell$.
- **Soundness.** If $C$ _doesn't_ vanish identically on the cube, then $\widetilde{C}$ vanishes _almost nowhere_; i.e., it vanishes on a proportion consisting of _at most_ $\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}_\tau}|$ among $\mathcal{T}_\tau^\ell$'s points.

Thus it's complete and sound for the verifier to sample a random $r \gets \mathcal{T}_\tau^\ell$, and check whether $\widetilde{C}(r) \stackrel{?}= 0$.

## The Sumcheck

How can the verifier do this efficiently? The trick is to write down a polynomial expression for $\widetilde{C}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$. We've [already seen](/primer#multilinear-extensions) how to construct multilinear extensions:

$$\widetilde{C}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) = \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} C(v) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}, v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - 1})$$.

We now see that the verifier's main question $\widetilde{C}(r) \stackrel{?}= 0$ in turn amounts to the sum claim

$$0 \stackrel{?}= \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} C(v) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r_0, \ldots , r_{\ell - 1}, v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - 1})$$.

This is exactly the kind of thing we can sumcheck; indeed, by sumchecking the degree-two product of multilinears

$$(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) \mapsto C(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r_0, \ldots , r_{\ell - 1}, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$$,

the verifier may reduce the above sum claim to two _evaluation claims_ at some random point $r' \in \mathcal{T}_\tau^\ell$—namely, to claims about the evaluations $t(r')$ and $\widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r, r')$. To resolve the first of these, the verifier may simply query $t$ directly. To resolve the second, the verifier may compute $\widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r, r')$ locally (since the equality indicator polynomial is transparent!).
