# The Rest [The univariate skip's positive rounds]

As of the end of the [univariate skip](/reductions/zerocheck/univariate), the parties have obtained an _updated_ sum claim $S_0 \coloneqq g(s')$. Moreover, the prover has locally specialized his rectangular multilinears $\widehat{t}_0, \ldots , \widehat{t}_{n - 1}$. That is, for each $\widehat{t}_i$, using the verifier's univariate challenge $s'$, the prover has specialised $\widehat{t}_i(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1})$ to $\widehat{t}_i(s', X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1})$. This latter polynomial—in fact, it's a multilinear!—requires just $2^{\ell - k}$ values to represent, as opposed to the $2^\ell$ we started with.

Moreover, the claim we're interested in checking is simply that:

$$S_0 \stackrel{?}= \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell} \widehat{C}(s', v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - k - 1}) \cdot \delta_D(s', s) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(v, r)$$.

This is mechanically identical to a _normal, rectangular_ sumcheck claim, pertaining now to the $\ell - k$-variate multilinears $\widehat{t}_i(s', X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1})$ and the usual composition polynomial $c(Z_0, \ldots , Z_{n - 1})$. The prover and verifier may thus process it accordingly, by running the sumcheck protocol for $\ell - k$ rounds.

## The End

By proceeding in this way for $\ell - k$ rounds, the prover and verifier may reduce their initial claim to an _evaluation claim_ of the form $S_{\ell - k} \stackrel{?}= \widehat{C}(s', r'_0, \ldots , r'_{\ell - k - 1}) \cdot \delta_D(s', s) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r', r)$, for some point $r' \in \mathcal{T}_\tau^{\ell - k}$ sampled during the course of the standard sumcheck. Of course, the verifier may locally evaluate $\delta_D(s', s) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(r', r)$ himself. It thus remains for the verifier to learn $\widehat{C}(s', r'_0, \ldots , r'_{\ell - k - 1})$; to do this, it suffices in turn for the verifier to learn the evaluations $\widehat{t}_i(s', r'_0, \ldots , r'_{\ell - k - 1})$ for each input index $i \in \{0, \ldots , n - 1\}$ (having gotten these, he can just apply the composition polynomial $c(Z_0, \ldots , Z_{n - 1})$ upon them).

Where this gets tricky is that our evaluation claims on the initial input polynomials are _rectangular_, not standard. That is, if we instead needed to evaluate the standard cube-shaped multilinears $t_i(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ at some point, we would be fine—we would just feed that claim directly into our PCS. As it stands, we rather need to evaluate the _rectangularized_ small-field multilinears $\widehat{t}_i(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1})$, namely at the point $(s', r'_0, \ldots , r'_{\ell - k - 1})$.

This latter sort of rectangular evaluation _can_ be achieved by means of a blackbox reduction to evaluation claims on the underlying standard, cube-shaped multilinears $t_i(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$. As it happens, we can do better: by modifying in a minor—though white-box—way our [polynomial commitment scheme](/cryptography), we are able to _directly_ repurpose that scheme as an evaluation protocol for _rectangularized_ multilinears. We sketch the details of that modification [here](/cryptography/rectangular).
