# Univariate Zerocheck [A small-field zerocheck protocol, implementing the univariate skip with batching]

_Note: this will have to be rewritten, and most will probably be cut. We care more about the verifier than the prover._

The _univariate skip_ plays a key role in our small-field zerocheck protocol. The essential premise is that the multilinears upon which our composition polynomials act are, often, defined over small fields. This fact makes the 0th round of the zerocheck special, in that much of the computation required by that round may be carried out over some appropriate small field. In the zerocheck's positive-indexed rounds, on the other hand, the polynomials at hand "saturate" to the maximal field size, and the small-field advantage disappears.

The _univariate skip_ is designed to trade off 0th-round computation for positive-round computation. That is, the univariate skip serves to move as much work as possible into the 0th round; it vastly reduces, in the process, the amount of work which must be performed in the subsequent, positive-indexed rounds. Our univariate skip prover resides [here](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/protocols/sumcheck/prove/univariate.rs?ref_type=heads); we will refer to that file when possible. The fundamental ideas in this page are due to [[Gruen]](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/108). In Binius, we expand Gruen's ideas somewhat, by treating the _batch adjudication_ of many zerocheck claims, a setting which introduces complications.

## Background and Notation

To explain how our univariate skip operates, we introduce notation. We fix a binary field $K$ and a handful of multilinears $M_0, \ldots , M_{t - 1}$, each with $\ell$ variables and defined over $K$, say. We moreover fix a handful of $t$-variate _composition polynomials_ $C_j(Z_0, \ldots , Z_{t - 1}) \in K[Z_0, \ldots , Z_{t - 1}]$, for $j \in \{0, \ldots c - 1\}$, say. The _zerocheck claim_ entails that, for each $j \in \{0, \ldots , c - 1\}$ and each $v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell$, $C_j(M_0(v), \ldots , M_{t - 1}(v)) = 0$. In other words, the each of the compositions

$$C_j(M_0(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}), \ldots , M_{t - 1}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}))$$

vanishes identically on the cube $\mathcal{B}_\ell$. We note that, in general, we allow compositions $C_j$ with different _total degrees_ $d_j$. We write $d^* \coloneqq \max_{j = 0}^{c - 1} d_j$. Finally, we write $K \subset L$ for a cryptographically large field extension.

The _standard_ zerocheck would reduce this zerocheck claim claim to _evaluation_ claims on the individual multilinears $M_i$ (at some random point in $L^\ell$ sampled during the course of the zerocheck). We are going to opt for a different path. We're going to fix a nonnegative integer parameter $k \in \{1, \ldots , \ell\}$—our "skipping parameter"—as well as a domain $D \subset L$ of size $2^k$. (For the purposes of intuition, one can take $k = 7$ and $\ell = 20$, let's say.) Here, for technical reasons, we take $D \subset L$ to be an $\mathbb{F}_2$-vector subspace of $L$. We're going to introduce further polynomials $\widehat{M}_0(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1}), \ldots , \widehat{M}_{t - 1}(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1})$—each $\ell - k + 1$-variate—designed so as to take the same values that the multilinears $M_0(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}), \ldots , M_{t - 1}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ respectively do (but on $D \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k} \subset L^{\ell - k + 1}$, as opposed to on $\mathcal{B}_\ell \subset L^\ell$). More precisely, we identify $\mathcal{B}_k \cong D$ by associating $(u_0, \ldots , u_{k - 1}) \mapsto \sum_{i = 0}^{k - 1} u_i \cdot \beta_i$, where $(\beta_0, \ldots , \beta_{k - 1})$ is an $\mathbb{F}_2$-basis of $D$; we then define the polynomials $\widehat{M}_i$ in such a way as to make

$$\widehat{M}_i(u, v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - k - 1}) = M_i(u_0, \ldots , u_{k - 1}, v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - k - 1})$$

hold for each $i \in \{0, \ldots , t - 1\}$ and each $(u_0, \ldots , u_{k - 1}, v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - k - 1}) \in \mathcal{B}_k \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k} \cong D \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}$. Here, each polynomial $\widehat{M}_i(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k  - 1})$ is moreover of degree less than $2^k$ in its first variable $Y$ and linear in each of its subsequent variables. (Each such polynomial is uniquely captured by its table of values on $D \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}$.)

We note that our _desired_ zerocheck claim is equivalent to the claim whereby, for each $j \in \{0, \ldots , c - 1\}$, the composition

$$C_j(\widehat{M}_0(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1}), \ldots , \widehat{M}_{t - 1}(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1}))$$

vanishes identically on the oblong domain $D \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k} \subset L^{\ell - k + 1}$.

The point of the _univariate zerocheck_ is to target this latter (equivalent) claim instead, and indeed to reduce it to a claim about the values of the _rectangular_ polynomials $\widehat{M}_i$ at some point $(s', r'_0, \ldots , r'_{\ell - k - 1}) \in L^{\ell - k + 1}$, itself again sampled during the (univariate) zerocheck. It turns out that this latter procedure can be carried out more efficiently than the standard zerocheck can. The catch is that we need a way for the verifier to ascertain the values of the ultimate evaluations of the "imagined" rectangular polynomials $\widehat{M}_i(s', r'_0, \ldots , r'_{\ell - k - 1})$, given only standard commitments to the $M_i$. We will deal with both of these below.

## The Math

### Batching

Up to a soundness error of at most $\frac{t - 1}{|L|}$, it is enough for the verifier to rather sample a random batching constant $\alpha \gets L$, and to check that the single polynomial

$$\sum_{j = 0}^{c - 1} \alpha^j \cdot C_j(\widehat{M}_0(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1}), \ldots , \widehat{M}_{t - 1}(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1}))$$

vanishes identically on $D \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}$. We write $C^*(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1})$ for this polynomial.

### Unique low-degree extension

There is a unique $\ell - k + 1$-variate polynomial, say $\widetilde{C}^*(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1})$, which agrees identically with $C^*(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1})$ on $D \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}$, and which moreover is of degree less than $2^k$ in $Y$ and linear in each of its subsequent variables. In fact, we can write down an explicit expression for $\widetilde{C}^*(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1})$: this thing is exactly

$$\sum_{(u, v) \in D \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}} C^*(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1}) \cdot \delta_D(u, Y) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(v, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1}).$$

Here, we write $\delta_D(Y_0, Y_1)$ for the unique bivariate polynomial, of individual degree less than $2^k$ in each variable, whose restriction to $D \times D \subset L \times L$ is the equality indicator function.

Our second observation—which mirrors the theory behind the classical zerocheck—is that $\widetilde{C}^*(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1})$ vanishes identically on $D \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}$ _if and only if_ it vanishes on all of $L^{\ell - k + 1}$; more importantly, if it _doesn't_ vanish identically on $D \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}$, then it can vanish on at most a proportion of $\frac{2^k - 1 + \ell - k}{|L|}$ of $L^{\ell - k + 1}$'s points (this is just Schwartz–Zippel). It is thus both complete and sound for the verifier to pick a _random_ element $(s, r_0, \ldots , r_{\ell - k - 1}) \gets L^{\ell - k + 1}$, and to check whether $\widetilde{C}^*(s, r_0, \ldots , r_{\ell - k - 1}) \stackrel{?}= 0$.

### Oblong sumcheck

This this latter equality in turn amounts to an (oblong) sumcheck: indeed, it amounts to the claim whereby the sum, taken over $(u, v) \in D \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}$, of

$$C^*(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1}) \cdot \delta_D(Y, s) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1}, r)$$

is zero.

It follows just as in the normal sumcheck that the verifier can adjudicate this claim by the aid of a sequence of $\ell - k + 1$ _round polynomials_. The first among these will be the univariate polynomial

$$g^{**}(Y) \coloneqq \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}} C^*(Y, v) \cdot \delta_D(Y, s) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(v, r).$$

(Our naming conventions will become clear shortly.) This thing will be of degree as high as $d^* \cdot (2^k - 1) + (2^k - 1) = (d^* + 1) \cdot (2^k - 1)$ in $Y$. To convey $g^{**}(Y)$ to the verifier, it's thus enough for the prover to compute its value on more than $(d^* + 1) \cdot (2^k - 1)$ _total_ points. We write $D^{**} \subset L$ for an "extended" domain, containing more than $(d^* + 1) \cdot (2^k - 1)$ points. We note that in fact $g^{**}(Y)$ will vanish identically on $D$, since , $C^*(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1})$ vanishes identically on $D \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}$. It thus suffices for the prover to compute the value of $g^{**}(y)$ for each $y \in D^{**} \setminus D$.

On the other hand, we are going to introduce a few optimizations, to make our computation of $g^{**}(Y)$ more efficient.

### Excising the Lagrange polynomial

The first is a trick inspired by [[Gruen, § 3.2]](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/108). The idea is that the prover can rather evaluate the simpler polynomial

$$g^*(Y) \coloneqq \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}} C^*(Y, v) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(v, r)$$

on more than $(d^* + 1) \cdot (2^k - 1)$ points, and later "multiply in" $\delta_D(Y, s)$ pointwise.

We note further that $g^*(Y)$ is of lower degree than $g^{**}(Y)$ is, a fact that we can exploit. Indeed, to capture $g^*(Y)$ exhaustively, we must merely learn its value on more than $d^* \cdot (2^k - 1)$ points. We write $D^*$ for an _intermediate_ domain that contains each of the individual domains $D_j$ (_a fortiori_, $D^*$ will be of size more than $d^* \cdot (2^k - 1)$; we recall that $d^* \coloneqq \max_{j = 0}^{c - 1} d_j$). We note that $g^*(Y)$ too will vanish on $D$. We may thus compute $g^*(Y)$ for each $y \in D^* \setminus D$, and then _extrapolate_ the result onto the larger domain $D^{**} \setminus D$. The point of course is that the work of this extrapolation takes place out of the "hot loop"; it's much easier to extend $g^*(Y)$ onto $D^{**} \setminus D$ this way than by directly using the sum-based definition above.

### Lifting the batching combination

We are going to do a further optimization whereby $g^*(Y)$ is computed "in pieces", independently for each $j \in \{0, \ldots , c - 1\}$, and _only then_ batched. In short, we are going to compute _individually_ the round polynomials

$$g_j(Y) \coloneqq \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}} C_j(\widehat{M}_0(Y, v), \ldots , \widehat{M}_{t - 1}(Y, v)) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(v, r).$$

We note that $g^*(Y) = \sum_{j = 0}^{c - 1} \alpha^j \cdot g_j(Y)$ holds. Provided we can compute _each individual_ polynomial $g_j(Y)$—say, in the sense of obtaining its evaluations over the set $D_j \setminus D$—we can then multiply these polynomials by the appropriate powers of $\alpha$, interpolate them individually, and finally add together the results, to get $g^*(Y)$. We can then evaluate this combination $g^*(Y)$ over $D^{**} \setminus D$, which—as we just saw above—is enough to achieve what we want.

"Lifting the combination" in this way actually gives us two, completely distinct advantages:

- It lets us lift the the powers-of-$\alpha$ combination _out_ of the "hot loop"—we only have to compute this combination on the order of $|D^* \setminus D|$ times, as opposed to $2^{\ell - k} \cdot |D^* \setminus D|$ times. The tradeoff is that we now have to maintain as much as $\sum_{j = 0}^{c - 1} |D_j \setminus D|$ total space overhead during the process, as opposed to just $|D^* \setminus D|$, but this is small change.
- The respective individual polynomials $g_j(Y)$ are, generally, of _different_ degrees (since the $d_j$s are allowed to be unequal). These differences get washed over when we batch. In particular, in order to uniquely determine $g_j(Y)$, it's enough merely to determine its values on more than $d_j \cdot (2^k - 1)$ points, as opposed to on more than $d^* \cdot (2^k - 1)$ points. This means that we must only evaluate the composition $C_j$ in the hot loop something like $2^{\ell - k} \cdot \left( d_j \cdot (2^k - 1) - 2^k \right)$ times, in order to exhaustively determine $g_j(Y)$, as opposed to the $2^{\ell - k} \cdot \left( d^* \cdot (2^k - 1) - 2^k \right)$ evaluations that would be required to learn $g^*(Y)$. (We can subtract $2^k$ points from these expressions, since we don't need to evaluate on $D$.)

So algorithmically, we are going to, for each $j \in \{0, \ldots , c - 1\}$, evaluate $g_j(Y)$ identically over $y \in D_j \setminus D$, where $D_j$ is some domain containing _more than_ $d_j \cdot (2^k - 1)$ points. We will then need to $\alpha$-scale these polynomials' evaluation vectors, interpolate them individually, add them, and then extrapolate the result onto $D^{**} \setminus D$. This will give us $g^*(Y)$; finally, we will multiply the result pointwise by $\delta_D(Y, s)$ on $D^{**} \setminus D$, in order to get $g^{**}(Y)$.

## The Protocol

Here, we summarize the above developments in a more algorithmically concrete way, emphasizing efficiency considerations.

### Extrapolating the initial data

As was hinted at above, we'll begin by picking, for each $j \in \{0, \ldots , c - 1\}$, an extended domain $D_j \subset L$ which _contains_ $D$ and moreover is of size greater than $d_j \cdot (2^k - 1)$. In practice, we will actually "round up" each $D_j$ by adding $d_j - 1$ points to it, so that it becomes rather of size $d_j \cdot 2^k$. Further, we will want each domain $D_j$ to be a union of cosets of our original domain $D$. We will finally pick domain $D^*$ as before; we can actually just let $D^* \coloneqq \bigcup_{j = 0}^{c - 1} D_j$.

Our first task will be to extrapolate _all_ of our oblong polynomials $\widehat{M}_i(Y, X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1})$ onto the larger domain $D^* \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k} \subset L^{\ell - k + 1}$. Concretely, this looks like taking, for each $i \in \{0, \ldots , t - 1\}$, the table of values of $\widehat{M}_i$, partitioning that table into $2^{\ell - k}$ chunks of size $2^k$, and then, in parallel, extrapolating each chunk from $D$ to $D^*$. That is, we interpret each chunk as the _values_ of some unique polynomial of degree less than $2^k$ on $D$ (in fact, this polynomial is nothing other than $\widehat{M}_i(Y, v)$, where $v \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}$ is a chunk index), and then extrapolate that polynomial to $D^*$. This task can itself be achieved efficiently—that is, in $\Theta(k \cdot |D^*|)$ time, per-chunk—using the additive NTT [[LCH14]](https://arxiv.org/abs/1404.3458). Put yet a different way, we are doing $2^{\ell - k}$ independent, parallel, systematic Reed–Solomon encodings, each from a domain of size $2^k$ to a domain of size $d^* \cdot 2^k$.

### Evaluating the composition polynomials

Having done this, we will now proceed through our composition polynomials $j \in \{0, \ldots , c - 1\}$. For each such $j$, we will evaluate $g_j(y)$ (see above) for each $y \in D_j \setminus D$. Note that, for each $v \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}$, the "substrate" data $\widehat{M}_0(y, v), \ldots , \widehat{M}_{t - 1}(y, v)$—i.e., upon which the composition $C_j(Z_0, \ldots , Z_{t - 1})$ must be evaluated—are already available on-hand to us (by design, in light of the extrapolation we just computed and the fact that $D_j \subset D^*$). It will thus remain merely to evaluate the composition $C_j$ on a bunch of ready-made points (something like $2^{\ell - k} \cdot (d_j - 1) \cdot 2^k = 2^\ell \cdot (d_j - 1)$ times).

We thus see why we evaluated the $\widehat{M}_i$s onto $D^*$—we wanted to prepare "in advance" everything we would need in order to evaluate the $C_j$s.

### Interpolating, combining, and extrapolating

After doing this, the prover will have, for each $j \in \{0, \ldots , c - 1\}$, the _values_ of $g_j(Y)$ over $D_j$. The prover will then need to $\alpha$-scale these things (i.e., for each $j \in \{0, \ldots , c - 1\}$, multiply $g_j(Y)$'s evaluations pointwise by $\alpha^j$). This will take $(d_j - 1) \cdot 2^k$ multiplications per polynomial (here, we exploit the fact that $g_j(Y)$ is nonzero only over $D_j \setminus D$). Finally, the prover will interpolate each of the resulting (scaled) evaluation vectors into the coefficient domain. Though this task takes place outside of the "hot loop"—and so is less performance-criticial—we note that it too can be carried out efficiently, using a certain generalization of the inverse additive NTT designed to treat domains whose sizes contain odd cofactors.

At this point, the prover may add together the appropriately scaled polynomials $g_j(Y)$ (i.e., in the coefficient domain).

Finally, the prover must evaluate the _combination_ $g^*(Y) = \sum_{j = 0}^{c - 1} \alpha^j \cdot g_j(Y)$ on $D^{**} \setminus D$. This can itself be achieved efficiently using a single forward NTT on the domain $D^{**}$. At this point, the prover may multiply pointwise by $\delta_D(Y, s)$ on $D^{**} \setminus D$, thereby obtaining $g^{**}(Y)$.

This completes the specification of the prover's computation of $g^{**}(Y)$.

## The verifier's challenge and beyond

Upon receiving $g^{**}(Y)$, the verifer may finally sample $s' \gets L$ and send it to the prover. Locally, the verifier will need to check that $g^{**}(y) = 0$ for each $y \in D$; in practice, the prover won't even send these points (so this will be true by _fiat_). The verifier will then update its running sum claim to $a \coloneqq g^{**}(s')$.

Up to a soundness error of $\frac{(d^* + 1) \cdot 2^k}{|L|}$ over the verifier's choice of $s' \in L$, our task has thus been reduced to showing that

$$a \stackrel{?}= \sum_{v \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}} C^*(s', X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1}) \cdot \delta_D(s', s) \cdot \widetilde{\texttt{eq}}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1}, r).$$

This latter thing is a perfectly good $\ell - k$-variate sumcheck over $L$, and can be handled using the [usual algorithms](/) for that task.

### Specializing the rectangular polynomials

We note that the prover will need to compute, for each $i \in \{0, \ldots , t - 1\}$, the table of values of the specialized polynomial $\widehat{M}_i(s', X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - k - 1})$ over $\mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}$. This specialization itself will entail performing an independent barycentric extrapolation, for each $v \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}$, of the $2^k$ datapoints $\widehat{M}_i(y, v)$ (here, $y$ varies throughout $D$). This extrapolation can be carried out in linear time, and involves only small-field operations.

### The very end

At the very end, the verifier will need to ascertain the respective values, for $i \in \{0, \ldots , t - 1\}$, of $\widehat{M}_i(s', r'_0, \ldots , r'_{\ell - k - 1})$, for certain further challenges $(r'_0, \ldots , r'_{\ell - k - 1})$ sampled during the course of the positive-indexed rounds of the zerocheck. Getting these values requires a bit of trickery, since our standard PCS evaluation scheme only lets us evaluate the standard $M_i$s (without the hats) on points in $L^\ell$. As we argue in our section on [ring-switching](/), the $\widehat{M}_i$ too can be evaluated efficiently, though the details are subtle.


## Efficiency Matters

The key point is that the entire "univariate skip" procedure above can be carried out over various small fields. The full picture is complicated, since there are many different fields at play (at least _a priori_). Indeed, the underlying multilinears $M_i(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1})$ could even be defined over different fields. Barring that, the composition polynomials $C_j$ also could be. To simplify our analysis below, we assume that all of the $M_i$s and $C_j$s are defined over the single field $K$. Finally, we write $K^*$ and $K^{**}$, respectively, for the minimally large tower fields large enough to contain $D^*$ and $D^{**}$. In practice, we will choose the $\mathbb{F}_2$-vector subspaces $D^*$ and $D^{**}$ of $L$ in such a way that they "play nicely" with $L$'s field structure. That is, we will design these subspaces in such a way as to be contained in as small a subfield of $L$ as possible. (This is easy to do provided we choose an $\mathbb{F}_2$-basis of $L$ such that all of $L$'s subfields are generated by appropriate prefixes of the basis. The tower basis described in [[DP23, § 2.3]](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1784) has this property.)

We sketch the key sources of efficiency gain as follows. For each $j \in \{0, \ldots , c - 1\}$, the extrapolation of the polynomials $\widehat{M}_i(Y, v)$ (for varying $v \in \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}$) from $D$ to $D^*$ can be carried out entirely over the _larger_ of the two fields $K$ and $K^*$. Moreover, when these fields differ, further efficiency gains can be realized. (For example, if $K = \mathbb{F}_2$, then we must merely extrapolate a _bit-vector_ over $K^*$; this task is more efficient than extrapolating a $K^*$-vector is.) Similarly, the evaluation of the constraint polynomial $C_j$ over the entirety of $D^* \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}$ can be carried out exclusively over the larger among the fields $K$ and $K^*$.

The precise field required is a bit subtle. Indeed, we write $K$ as before for our polynomials' coefficient field. For simplicity, we assume that each composition polynomial $C_j$ is also defined over $K$, as well as that $K$ is sufficiently large that it can be made to contain $D^{**}$.
