# Univariate Zerocheck [A small-field zerocheck protocol, implementing the univariate skip]

The _univariate skip_ plays a key role in our small-field zerocheck protocol. The essential premise is that the multilinears upon which our composition polynomials act are, often, defined over small fields. This fact makes the 0th round of the zerocheck special, in that much of the computation required by that round may be carried out over some appropriate small field. In the zerocheck's positive-indexed rounds, on the other hand, the polynomials at hand "saturate" to the maximal field size, and the small-field advantage disappears.

The _univariate skip_ is designed to trade off 0th-round computation for positive-round computation. That is, the univariate skip serves to move as much work as possible into the 0th round; it vastly reduces, in the process, the amount of work which must be performed in the subsequent, positive-indexed rounds. Our univariate skip prover resides [here](https://gitlab.com/IrreducibleOSS/binius/-/blob/main/crates/core/src/protocols/sumcheck/prove/univariate.rs?ref_type=heads); we will refer to that file when possible. The fundamental ideas in this page are due to [[Gruen]](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/108).

## Background and Notation

To explain how our univariate skip operates, we introduce notation. We fix a large binary field $L$, a subfield $K \subset L$, and a handful of multilinears $M_0, \ldots , M_{t - 1}$, each with $\ell$ variables and defined over $K$, say. We fix moreover a $t$-variate _composition polynomial_ $C(Y_0, \ldots , Y_{t - 1}) \in K[Y_0, \ldots , Y_{t - 1}]$. The _zerocheck claim_ entails that, for each $v \in \mathcal{B}_\ell$, $C(M_0(v), \ldots , M_{t - 1}(v)) = 0$. In other words, the composition

$$C(M_0(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}), \ldots , M_{t - 1}(X_0, \ldots , X_{\ell - 1}))$$

vanishes identically on the cube $\mathcal{B}_\ell$.

The _standard_ zerocheck would reduce this particular claim to _evaluation_ claims on the individual multilinears $M_i$ (at some random point $r \in L^\ell$ sampled during the course of the zerocheck). We are going to opt for a different path. We're going to fix a nonnegative integer parameter $k \in \{0, \ldots , \ell\}$—our "skipping parameter"—as well as a domain $D \subset L$ of size $2^k$. (For the purposes of intuition, one can take $k = 7$.) We're going to "imagine" polynomials $\widehat{M}_0, \ldots , \widehat{M}_{t - 1}$—each defined on the _rectangular_ domain $D \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k} \subset L^{\ell - k + 1}$—which take the same values that the multilinears $M_0, \ldots , M_{t - 1}$ respectively do (but on _this_ domain, instead of the $\ell$-cube). That is, provided we identify $\mathcal{B}_k \cong D$ by associating $(u_0, \ldots , u_{k - 1}) \mapsto \sum_{i = 0}^{k - 1} u_i \cdot \beta_i$—where $(\beta_0, \ldots , \beta_{k - 1})$ is an $\mathbb{F}_2$-basis of $D$—we obtain the identity

$$\widehat{M}_i(u, v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - k - 1}) = M_i(u_0, \ldots , u_{k - 1}, v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - k - 1})$$

for each $i \in \{0, \ldots , t - 1\}$ and each $(u_0, \ldots , u_{k - 1}, v_0, \ldots , v_{\ell - k - 1}) \in \mathcal{B}_k \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k} \cong D \times \mathcal{B}_{\ell - k}$.
