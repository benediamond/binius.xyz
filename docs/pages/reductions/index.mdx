# Cryptographic Reductions [Reducing multiset-equality and vanishing claims to evaluation claims]
 
In this section, we describe Binius's cryptographic reductions. These reductions constitute a sort of "conveyor belt" or "middleware", and occupy a critical intermediate territory in our system.

In prime-field SNARKs based on the _DEEP-ALI_ paradigm—like [Plonky3](https://github.com/Plonky3/Plonky3) and [Stwo](https://github.com/starkware-libs/stwo)—the need for _cryptographic reductions_ like ours doesn't quite exist. Those schemes essentially support _just_ tables, and not channels; they moreover contain native mechanisms that serve to reduce vanishing claims to evaluation claims.

In Binius, we must enrich this model, for various reasons. First, we rely heavily on _virtual polynomials_, polynomials which "sit on top of"—and modify—underlying committed polynomials. Virtual polynomials may be compositions or concatenations, say, of raw polynomials; they may also modify their underlying polynomials in more-complex ways—by _shifting_ or _packing_ them, for example. The _virtual polynomial_ abstraction appears implicitly in [_HyperPlonk_](https://eprint.iacr.org/2022/1355), and is developed heavily in Section 4 of [_Succinct Arguments in Towers of Binary Fields_](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1784).

Our tables, in general, may contain _both_ committed and virtual columns. The gate constraints which act on these tables, therefore, act in turn upon complex mixes, consisting of both committed and virtual columns. After zerochecking the composition polynomial associated with any particular such gate constraint, we thus wind up with _evaluation claims_ on the composition's underlying polynomials (both committed _and_ virtual). In this section, we discuss the portions of Binius which reduce evaluation claims on _virtual_ polynomials to further evaluation claims on _committed_ polynomials. Binius achieves this by progressively "unrolling" the definitions of the virtual polynomials at hand. Since these definition often themselves involve sums, sumchecks ensue. Binius must take responsibility for scheduling, orchestrating, and batching the requisite sumchecks—in such a manner that the prover and verifier can coordinate.

Our use of channels, or _multi-multiset matching_, necessitates that we deal with a further sort of reduction. Indeed, multi-multiset matching outputs _multiset equality_ claims. The multisets at hand—i.e., which each such claim operates on—are themselves virtual; in general, they're complex concatenations of other polynomials, _which themselves_ are virtual in general. Our cryptographic reduction layer, therefore, must moreover handle multiset-equality claims; it must reduce these claims to _grand-product claims_, and—via GKR—ultimately to evaluation claims.

## Section Directory

This section contains the following subsections:
- [**Zerocheck.**](zerocheck) Zerochecking is the outermost layer of our conveyor belt of reductions, as least as far as vanishing claims are concerned. This step intakes a _zerocheck claim_—that is, a claim whereby a certain composition of multilinears vanishes identically over the cube—and reduces that claim to a set of _evaluation claims_, themselves pertaining to the various multilinears (both raw and virtual) underlying the initial composition. In this setting, we use sophisticated techniques tailored to the case of _small-field polynomials_—a research area initiated by Angus Gruen's [_Some Improvements for the PIOP for ZeroCheck_](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/108).
